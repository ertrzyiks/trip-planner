## Why this place matters

The liberation of Paris was not militarily necessary in August 1944 — Eisenhower had intended to bypass the city, fearing it would become a logistics burden — but it was politically and symbolically unavoidable. The French Forces of the Interior (FFI) rose in insurrection on 19 August, and the French political reality made it impossible for Allied forces to leave a liberated France while Paris remained under German control. General de Gaulle pressed Eisenhower to commit the French 2nd Armored Division (General Leclerc), and on 24–25 August 1944 Leclerc's division entered Paris alongside the US 4th Infantry Division.

The German military governor, General Dietrich von Choltitz, had received Hitler's explicit order to demolish Paris — its bridges, monuments, and infrastructure — before surrendering. He did not carry out the order. The reasons he gave vary in his own accounts, and historians debate how much of his decision reflected personal conscience and how much a calculation that the war was lost and destruction served no military purpose. The result was that Paris survived with its fabric almost entirely intact.

De Gaulle's march down the Champs-Élysées on 26 August 1944, under sniper fire that broke out at the Place de la Concorde, was photographed and filmed and became one of the defining images of the liberation of France.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-08-19: FFI resistance fighters begin insurrection in Paris; police strike and partial German withdrawal create a volatile situation.
- 1944-08-22: Eisenhower, under pressure from de Gaulle, authorizes the advance on Paris; French 2nd Armored Division (General Philippe Leclerc) and US 4th Infantry Division are directed toward the city.
- 1944-08-24 evening: First Leclerc column (Captain Dronne's Nueve Company, largely Spanish Republican veterans) reaches the Hôtel de Ville; the bells of Notre-Dame ring across Paris.
- 1944-08-25: Main French 2nd Armored and US 4th Infantry Division columns enter the city; General von Choltitz signs the surrender of the Paris garrison at the Préfecture de Police; the city is liberated with its monuments intact; German losses estimated at 3,200 killed and 12,800 prisoners.
- 1944-08-26: General de Gaulle leads the victory march down the Champs-Élysées from the Arc de Triomphe to Notre-Dame; sniper fire breaks out but the procession continues.

## Important people

- General Philippe Leclerc: Commander French 2nd Armored Division; led the liberation force into Paris; his personal ambition to be the first to liberate the capital shaped the operation's political dynamics.
- General Dietrich von Choltitz: German Military Governor of Paris; received Hitler's order to destroy the city and declined to execute it; surrendered to Leclerc and the French at the Préfecture de Police on 25 August.
- General Charles de Gaulle: Leader of the Free French; his march down the Champs-Élysées on 26 August, and his speech at the Hôtel de Ville, established the narrative of French self-liberation that shaped French politics for decades.
- Captain Raymond Dronne: Led the Nueve Company of the French 2nd Armored — many of them Spanish Republican exiles — as the first Allied column to reach the Hôtel de Ville on the evening of 24 August.
- General Dietrich Eisenhower: Supreme Allied Commander; authorizing the Paris operation was a political more than military decision; his logistical concerns proved correct — feeding Paris diverted significant Allied supply capacity.

## What to see nearby

- Arc de Triomphe: De Gaulle's march on 26 August began here; the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier is below the arch.
- Hôtel de Ville: Where the first Leclerc column arrived on the night of 24 August; de Gaulle's liberation speech was delivered from the balcony on 25 August.
- Préfecture de Police (Île de la Cité): Where von Choltitz signed the surrender on 25 August; the insurrection began here on 19 August.
- Musée de l'Armée (Les Invalides): Major French military museum covering French military history including WW2 and the liberation campaign.
- Memorial de la Shoah (Marais district): Dedicated to the memory of the French Jews deported during the German occupation; essential complement to the liberation narrative.

## Museums

- Musée de l'Armée (Les Invalides, 7th arrondissement): The national French military museum; the WW2 galleries cover the fall of France in 1940, the Free French, and the liberation; allow 2 to 3 hours for the full museum.
- Mémorial de la France Combattante (Mont Valérien, Suresnes, western suburb): Site where the Germans executed 1,000 French resistance fighters during the occupation; the most significant site of occupation-era repression near Paris.
- Memorial de la Shoah (4th arrondissement): Memorial and museum covering the French Jewish community under the German occupation and the deportations to the death camps; essential for understanding what liberation meant for those who survived.

## Visitor notes

Paris connects the Normandy campaign to the broader liberation of France and is most powerful for WW2 visitors when approached through the occupation as much as the liberation. Start at the Memorial de la Shoah to understand what the occupation meant for Paris's population, then walk or drive the liberation route from the Arc de Triomphe to the Hôtel de Ville and the Préfecture. The Musée de l'Armée at Les Invalides deserves a half-day on its own. Mont Valérien, a 20-minute drive west, is the most sobering occupation site in the Paris region.
