## Why this place matters

Pegasus Bridge was the first objective of the entire D-Day operation, seized a full six hours before the beach landings began. In the opening minutes of 6 June 1944, three Horsa gliders carrying D Company, 2nd Battalion Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire Light Infantry (part of the 6th Airborne Division) landed within meters of the bridge over the Caen Canal at Bénouville. Within minutes of touchdown, the company had taken the bridge in a rushed assault before the German garrison could destroy it.

The operation mattered because the bridge — and a companion bridge over the Orne River 500 meters east — controlled the only road corridor on the eastern flank of the invasion zone. Without them in Allied hands, German armor could reinforce the beaches from Caen, and the 6th Airborne Division's paratroopers scattered across the Orne bridgehead would have no reliable link back to the beachhead. Lord Lovat's 1st Special Service Brigade Commandos, who marched from Sword Beach inland on D-Day afternoon while Piper Bill Millin played, relieved the bridges exactly on schedule.

The original bridge was replaced in 1994. The original span is displayed at the Mémorial Pegasus museum 200 meters away.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 00:16: Three Horsa gliders carrying D Company, 2nd Ox and Bucks (6th Airborne Division), land beside the Caen Canal bridge; Sergeant Jim Wallwork pilots the lead glider to within meters of the bridge.
- 1944-06-06 00:21: Major John Howard's men rush the bridge; Lieutenant Den Brotheridge is mortally wounded — the first Allied officer killed in action on D-Day — but the bridge is taken intact; German garrison is overwhelmed before they can detonate the explosive charges.
- 1944-06-06 ~00:30: A parallel assault on the Orne River bridge (later named Horsa Bridge) succeeds with similar speed; both crossings secured within 15 minutes of landing.
- 1944-06-06 morning: Paratroopers of the 7th Parachute Battalion and other 6th Airborne units arrive at the bridges, reinforcing D Company against German counterattacks.
- 1944-06-06 ~13:00: 1st Special Service Brigade Commandos, led by Lord Lovat with Piper Bill Millin playing his pipes, march from Sword Beach and cross Pegasus Bridge — the relief arrives roughly on the promised schedule.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-08-17: The bridges remain the only Allied-held crossing of the Caen Canal for months; German forces repeatedly probe the bridgehead but cannot retake the crossing.

## Important people

- Major John Howard: Commander D Company, 2nd Ox and Bucks; led the assault and defended the bridge until relief; the central figure in Cornelius Ryan and Stephen Ambrose's accounts of the operation.
- Sergeant Jim Wallwork: Pilot of the lead glider; landed at the target with extraordinary precision in complete darkness, winning the Distinguished Flying Medal.
- Lieutenant Den Brotheridge: Led the first rush across the bridge; shot in the neck and died shortly after — generally regarded as the first Allied officer killed in ground combat on D-Day.
- Lord Lovat (Brigadier Simon Fraser): Commander 1st Special Service Brigade; led the Commando march from Sword Beach to relieve the bridges, arriving exactly on schedule.
- Piper Bill Millin: Lord Lovat's personal piper; played the bagpipes as the Commandos crossed Pegasus Bridge, in deliberate violation of War Office regulations forbidding pipes in combat.

## What to see nearby

- Mémorial Pegasus (200 m east): Museum housing the original bridge span, a D Company jeep, weapons, and artifacts from the assault. The best companion site.
- Horsa Bridge (500 m east over the Orne): The second bridge taken in the same operation; less visited but part of the same assault.
- Ranville War Cemetery (1 km): The first Allied war cemetery established in France after D-Day; contains the grave of Lieutenant Den Brotheridge.
- Sword Beach (Ouistreham, 8 km north): The beach the Commandos marched inland from to relieve Pegasus Bridge.
- Merville Gun Battery (12 km northeast): The parallel 6th Airborne assault that silenced guns threatening Sword Beach.

## Museums

- Mémorial Pegasus (Ranville): The essential museum for this operation; displays the original 1934 bridge span, Howard's jeep, a reproduction Horsa glider, and personal accounts from D Company veterans. Closed in January; book ahead in peak summer season.

## Visitor notes

Allow 2 hours for the Mémorial Pegasus museum and a walk to the bridge itself. The café beside the bridge — Café Gondrée — is the first house liberated in France and still run by the Gondrée family; worth a stop. Combine with Sword Beach and Merville Gun Battery for a full British airborne and beach day. Ranville War Cemetery is a short walk and takes 20 minutes; find Den Brotheridge's grave in row B.
