## Why this place matters

Pointe du Hoc is the headland between Utah Beach and Omaha Beach where, on the morning of 6 June 1944, three battalions of the 2nd and 5th US Ranger Battalions executed one of the most audacious assaults of the entire Normandy campaign. Under German fire, Rangers scaled the 30-metre chalk cliffs using rocket-propelled grapnel hooks and rope ladders to destroy a battery of 155mm guns that Allied planners feared could range both invasion beaches simultaneously.

The guns were not in their casements when the Rangers arrived — they had been moved inland — but Ranger patrols found and destroyed them in an orchard hours later. The garrison held the promontory against German counterattacks for two days until relieved, by which time roughly half the assault force had been killed or wounded.

Today the site preserves a stark landscape of bomb craters, concrete casemates, and the original Ranger memorial, making it one of the most physically evocative D-Day sites in Normandy.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~07:10: 225 Rangers from the 2nd Ranger Battalion (Companies D, E, F) land at the base of the cliffs; a third battalion (5th Rangers plus Companies A and B of the 2nd) diverts to Omaha after the assault signal is not received on time.
- 1944-06-06 morning: Rangers reach the cliff top, neutralise the gun positions, and send patrols inland; a patrol from D Company locates and destroys the five 155mm howitzers in a camouflaged position roughly 1.5 km south.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-08: Isolated on the promontory, the Rangers repel multiple German counterattacks by elements of the 914th Grenadier Regiment (352nd Infantry Division); ammunition, food and water run critically low.
- 1944-06-08: Elements of the 116th Infantry Regiment (29th Division) break through and relieve the Rangers; of roughly 225 who landed on D-Day, fewer than 90 remained combat-effective.

## Important people

- Lt. Col. James E. Rudder: Commanding officer of the 2nd Ranger Battalion; landed with his men and commanded the defence of the clifftop position throughout the two-day siege.
- Sgt. Leonard Lomell: D Company Ranger who led the inland patrol that located and destroyed the howitzers, directly achieving the mission's primary objective.
- Sgt. Jack Kuhn: Partnered with Lomell on the patrol; used thermite grenades to render the guns permanently inoperable.
- Gen. Omar Bradley: US First Army commander who ordered the Ranger assault; famously said he would accept Pointe du Hoc as his primary D-Day concern.

## What to see nearby

- Pointe du Hoc Ranger Memorial (on site): The American Battle Monuments Commission preserves the craters, casemates, and observation bunker largely as they appeared in 1944; the site is free to visit.
- Omaha Beach and the Normandy American Cemetery (8 km east): Essential complement to understand the broader assault that the Ranger mission was designed to protect.
- Utah Beach Landing Museum (18 km west): Covers the other beachhead that the Pointe du Hoc battery threatened.
- Grandcamp-Maisy and the Maisy Battery: A larger German artillery complex nearby, only rediscovered and opened to the public in the 2000s; provides additional context on the coastal defence network.

## Museums

- Normandy American Cemetery and Memorial Visitor Center (Colleville-sur-Mer): The closest major interpretive centre with extensive coverage of the Omaha sector and Ranger operations.
- Utah Beach Landing Museum (Sainte-Marie-du-Mont): Covers the western flank that the Pointe du Hoc battery would have dominated.
- Overlord Museum (Colleville-sur-Mer): Armour and equipment museum immediately adjacent to the American Cemetery.

## Visitor notes

Allow 1 to 1.5 hours on site. The ground is uneven — the bomb craters are left unfilled as a deliberate preservation choice — so wear appropriate footwear. The observation bunker is accessible and offers a commanding view of both beach sectors. Combine with Omaha Beach and the American Cemetery for a full-day Omaha sector itinerary, or pair with Utah Beach and Sainte-Mère-Église for a US airborne and seaborne route.
