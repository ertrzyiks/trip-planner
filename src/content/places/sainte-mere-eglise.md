## Why this place matters

Sainte-Mère-Église holds the distinction of being the first town liberated on French soil during the Normandy campaign, secured by paratroopers of the 82nd Airborne Division in the early hours of 6 June 1944. The town sits astride Route Nationale 13, the main road running up the Cotentin Peninsula to Cherbourg, making it a critical chokepoint that US planners needed in friendly hands before the Utah Beach landings began at dawn.

The drop was chaotic. A fire in the town square was being fought by local residents under German supervision when American paratroopers began landing directly into the illuminated square. Several were shot before touching the ground or were trapped on rooftops and church towers. Private John Steele's parachute snagged on the church steeple; he hung there for two hours feigning death before being taken prisoner. Despite the disaster in the square, troopers landing in surrounding fields assembled and took the town by dawn.

The town became the first milestone of the western Allied advance, and the decision to stand and defend it against German counterattacks — rather than fall back — shaped the entire Cotentin campaign.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~00:15–02:30: Sticks from the 505th Parachute Infantry Regiment (82nd Airborne Division) scatter across the Cotentin Peninsula; several hundred men land near or in Sainte-Mère-Église; a burning building illuminates the drop zone and draws catastrophic fire onto paratroopers descending into the town.
- 1944-06-06 ~04:30: Lieutenant Colonel Edward "Cannonball" Krause leads troopers of 3rd Battalion, 505th PIR in a sweep of the town, killing or capturing the German garrison; the US flag raised before dawn; the town is the first in France to be liberated.
- 1944-06-06 morning: The 91st Infantry Division (German) and elements of the 6th Fallschirmjäger Regiment mount counterattacks to retake the town and the road north; 82nd Airborne troopers hold against repeated pressure.
- 1944-06-07: 4th Infantry Division (Utah Beach) pushes inland and relieves the 505th PIR garrison at Sainte-Mère-Église, ending its isolation.
- 1944-06-08 to 1944-06-12: VII Corps uses the town as a hub for the drive north toward Cherbourg and south to cut the Cotentin Peninsula.

## Important people

- Lieutenant Colonel Edward Krause ("Cannonball"): Commander 3rd Battalion, 505th PIR; led the assault that cleared the town before dawn; raised his personal American flag — the same flag he had raised in Naples — over the town hall.
- Private John Steele: 505th PIR paratrooper whose parachute caught on the church steeple; hung there feigning death for two hours; survived, was taken prisoner, escaped, and returned to US lines; his story became one of the most famous individual images of D-Day.
- General Matthew Ridgway: Commander 82nd Airborne Division; his division's scattered drop paradoxically confused the German response by spreading alarms across too wide an area for a coordinated defense.
- General Wilhelm Falley: Commander German 91st Infantry Division; killed in his staff car on 6 June while moving toward the airborne drop zones — one of the highest-ranking German officers lost on D-Day.

## What to see nearby

- Airborne Museum (in town): The primary museum for 82nd and 101st Airborne operations, with a C-47 and Waco glider in covered displays.
- Utah Beach (12 km south): The beach the paratroopers' drop was designed to protect; combine for a full US airborne-seaborne itinerary.
- Chef-du-Pont and La Fière bridge: Site of one of the most vicious small-unit actions of the Normandy campaign, where 82nd Airborne troopers held the Merderet River causeway for days against German armored counterattacks.
- Dead Man's Corner Museum (Saint-Côme-du-Mont): Covers 101st Airborne operations south of Sainte-Mère-Église; named for a German officer's body found at the roadside junction.

## Museums

- Airborne Museum (Sainte-Mère-Église): Two buildings cover 82nd and 101st Airborne operations; Building 1 (Utah) includes a preserved LCVP landing craft; Building 2 (WACO) houses a Waco CG-4A glider and C-47 Dakota suspended in a recreated night drop. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

## Visitor notes

The town is compact and walkable. The church with Steele's dummy paratrooper hanging from the steeple (a replica installed for visitors) is immediately identifiable. Allow half a day: the Airborne Museum plus a walk around the church, main square, and the liberation plaque on the town hall. Combine with Utah Beach and Pointe du Hoc for a full US sector day.
