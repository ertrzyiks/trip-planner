## Why this place matters

Douvres-la-Délivrande lay 5 km inland from Juno Beach. On D-Day, the Canadian 3rd Infantry Division swept past it on both sides, pushing toward Caen, but the fortified radar complex was too strong to take on the march. What followed was one of the more unusual episodes of the early campaign: a major German fortification sitting surrounded and bypassed, in Allied-held territory, for eleven days.

The station had been built to house a Würzburg-Riese radar array and a Freya long-range radar, together with the electronic warfare personnel and equipment to operate them. It was defended by concrete bunkers, wire obstacles, minefields, and a garrison of roughly 230 men — mostly Luftwaffe signals troops with some Wehrmacht infantry — who had no intention of surrendering to a passing column. Allied forces contained it with a small blocking force while the main advance continued, but it disrupted local communications and tied down troops that were needed elsewhere.

On 17 June, 41 Royal Marine Commando assaulted the position after a preliminary artillery bombardment. The bunkers and radar infrastructure survive as one of the most complete German electronic warfare sites preserved in western Europe, now open as a free outdoor museum.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06: Canadian 3rd Infantry Division bypasses the Douvres-la-Délivrande radar station on both flanks during its advance from Juno Beach; the garrison (Luftnachrichten-Regiment, Luftwaffe signals troops, approximately 230 men) refuses to surrender and seals the fortification.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-16: Allied forces maintain a containment perimeter around the station; German garrison remains active and in radio contact with German forces; the position sits 5 km inside Allied-held territory.
- 1944-06-17: 41 Royal Marine Commando (1st Special Service Brigade, 6th Airborne sector) assaults the position after artillery preparation; the garrison surrenders after a day's fighting; 227 prisoners taken.

## Important people

- Lieutenant Colonel Eric Palmer: Commanding officer 41 Royal Marine Commando; directed the assault that finally reduced the position on 17 June.
- General Rod Keller: Commander 3rd Canadian Infantry Division; his division bypassed Douvres on D-Day; the failure to reduce it on 6 June reflected the priority given to advancing toward Caen over reducing every strongpoint.

## What to see nearby

- Juno Beach (5 km north, Courseulles-sur-Mer and Bernières): The beach the 3rd Canadian Division landed on before sweeping past Douvres; the Juno Beach Centre is the primary museum.
- Sword Beach / Ouistreham (10 km northeast): The British beach sector from which 41 Commando operated; connects Douvres to the broader eastern flank story.
- Périers-sur-le-Dan (3 km west): The furthest point reached by the British 3rd Division on D-Day evening; the high-water mark of the first day's advance.
- Caen Memorial Museum (15 km south): The broader campaign context; Douvres illustrates how the rapid Canadian advance created pockets of German resistance that persisted for weeks.

## Museums

- Douvres-la-Délivrande Radar Museum (on site): Open-air museum at the original fortification; the two main Würzburg-Riese radar pedestals, bunker interiors, underground corridors, and surface fortifications are all accessible. The site preserves the only near-complete German radar installation of this type in Normandy. Free admission; no permanent staff — check local signage for access. Allow 45 to 60 minutes.

## Visitor notes

Douvres-la-Délivrande is one of Normandy's genuine hidden gems for visitors interested in technical military history or the German side of D-Day. The radar pedestals are impressive in scale, and the bunker interiors give an authentic feel of what the garrison of 230 men maintained for eleven days. The site is free, rarely crowded, and takes 45 to 60 minutes. Combine with Juno Beach and Bernières-sur-Mer (5 km north) for a Canadian sector morning, then drive east to Sword Beach and Pegasus Bridge in the afternoon.
