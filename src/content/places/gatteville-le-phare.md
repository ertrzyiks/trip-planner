## Why this place matters

Gatteville-le-Phare sits on a strategic section of the northeastern Cotentin coast, close to sea lanes and approaches that mattered to both German coastal defense planning and Allied post-D-Day operations. In June 1944, this coastline formed part of the wider battle space connected to the US advance toward Cherbourg.

While Gatteville itself was not a D-Day beach assault sector, it was tied to the Cotentin campaign that followed Operation Neptune. As US VII Corps pushed north, German command structures along the peninsula's coasts and ports were progressively isolated.

For WW2-focused visitors, the place helps connect battlefield narratives to the coastal surveillance and maritime-control dimension of Operation Overlord.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1940-1944: German occupation authorities and military commands integrated the northeastern Cotentin coast into Atlantic Wall-era surveillance and coastal defense planning.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-18: Following the Utah Beach landings, US First Army formations (notably VII Corps) expanded and secured the Cotentin lodgment while German Seventh Army elements attempted to delay the advance.
- 1944-06-19 to 1944-06-21: US VII Corps cut across Cotentin and isolated the northern peninsula, reducing the operational value of German coastal observation points in sectors including the Cap de la Hague to Barfleur arc.
- 1944-06-22 to 1944-06-27: US forces concentrated on the Battle of Cherbourg against entrenched German garrison and fortress units under mixed army-naval command.
- 1944-06 to 1944-07: After organized resistance collapsed in the north, Allied control of the coast supported follow-on logistics and coastal security operations; exact unit attribution varies by source for smaller local positions.

## Important people

- Lt. Gen. J. Lawton Collins: Commander of US VII Corps, led the campaign that isolated and seized the northern Cotentin zone.
- Gen. Omar Bradley: As US First Army commander, prioritized operations that secured Cotentin and opened the way for use of Cherbourg.
- Gen. Karl-Wilhelm von Schlieben: German commander associated with the final northern Cotentin defense centered on Cherbourg.
- Adm. Walter Hennecke: German naval commander involved in defense and demolition measures in the Cherbourg fortress-port system affecting the wider peninsula.

## What to see nearby

- Gatteville Lighthouse (Phare de Gatteville): A major landmark and viewpoint for understanding coastal observation lines and Channel approaches.
- Barfleur harbor area: Historic coastal townscape useful for reading the maritime geography of the northeastern peninsula.
- Saint-Vaast-la-Hougue and nearby fortifications: Vauban-era defenses that provide additional context on the long military history of this coast.
- Cherbourg and Fort du Roule: Essential stop for understanding how the Cotentin campaign culminated in capture of the key port.
- Azeville and Crisbecq batteries (day trip): Strong Atlantic Wall sites that complement this coastal-defense itinerary.

## Museums

- Liberation Museum at Fort du Roule (Cherbourg): Best regional museum link to the June 1944 fighting in northern Cotentin.
- Airborne Museum (Sainte-Mere-Eglise): Explains airborne operations that enabled wider US maneuver on the peninsula.
- Utah Beach Landing Museum: Good operational context for how the beachhead fed the northward drive toward Cherbourg.

## Visitor notes

Plan 1 to 2 hours for Gatteville-le-Phare itself, then pair it with either a Cherbourg-focused half day or a longer Cotentin route including Sainte-Mere-Eglise and Utah Beach. On clear days, prioritize elevated viewpoints to understand why this coastline mattered for wartime observation and control.
