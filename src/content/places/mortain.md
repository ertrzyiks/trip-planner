## Why this place matters

Mortain is a small hilltop town at the eastern edge of the Normandy bocage, commanding a high ridge above the Sée valley. After the American breakout at Avranches on 31 July 1944, Hitler ordered a concentrated counterattack westward — Operation Lüttich — with the goal of cutting through to the coast, severing Patton's supply lines, and turning the strategic situation around. Four German Panzer divisions struck toward Avranches in the pre-dawn hours of 7 August.

The attack initially caught the US 30th Infantry Division by surprise and penetrated the line, but it was stopped cold within 24 hours. The ridge at Mortain — Hill 317 — was held by the 1st Battalion, 120th Infantry Regiment, which was surrounded and cut off but refused to withdraw. The "Lost Battalion" on Hill 317 held for five days while subsisting on airdropped supplies, calling in artillery on German positions that were sometimes within grenade range. RAF Typhoons flying rocket attacks destroyed hundreds of German armored vehicles caught in the open during daylight. The counterattack stalled, and the forces that had attacked at Mortain were now further west and south than ever — perfectly positioned to be trapped in the Falaise Pocket when the Allied pincers closed.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-08-07 ~00:00: Operation Lüttich begins; 2nd SS Panzer Division "Das Reich," 116th Panzer Division, 1st SS Panzer Division "Leibstandarte," and 2nd Panzer Division attack westward toward Avranches; initial advance captures Mortain and penetrates 8 km.
- 1944-08-07 morning: 1st Battalion, 120th Infantry Regiment (30th Infantry Division) on Hill 317 is surrounded and cut off as German armor bypasses the ridge; the "Lost Battalion" begins its five-day defense.
- 1944-08-07–08: RAF Typhoons from 2nd Tactical Air Force fly hundreds of rocket-attack sorties against German armor in the open; post-war assessment finds Typhoon attacks halted the exploitation more than any ground action; exact claims disputed by historians.
- 1944-08-07 to 1944-08-12: US 30th Infantry Division counterattacks gradually restore the line; the "Lost Battalion" on Hill 317 holds out, resupplied by USAAF air drops; German forces fail to break through to Avranches.
- 1944-08-12: Relief columns reach the 1st Battalion on Hill 317; the battalion has suffered heavy casualties but held the critical observation point throughout.
- 1944-08-12–21: The Panzer divisions that attacked at Mortain are now committed far to the west, unable to withdraw east before the Falaise Pocket is closed around them.

## Important people

- Lieutenant Colonel Eads Hardaway: Commanding officer 1st Battalion, 120th Infantry Regiment; led the "Lost Battalion" through five days of encirclement on Hill 317.
- General J. Lawton Collins: Commander VII Corps (US First Army); organized the defense and counterattack that contained the Lüttich offensive.
- SS-Gruppenführer Paul Hausser: Commander German Seventh Army; carried out Hitler's Lüttich orders under protest, believing the attack was strategically reckless.
- General Omar Bradley: Commander US 12th Army Group; recognized that the Lüttich attack actually presented an opportunity — he turned Patton north rather than west, aiming to encircle the attacking German forces.

## What to see nearby

- Hill 317 viewpoint (northeast of Mortain): The high ridge where the "Lost Battalion" held; a memorial marks the position and the view over the bocage is extensive.
- Signal Hill (southwest of Mortain): A German observation point during Lüttich; the ridgeline gives context for the attacking direction.
- Avranches (25 km west): Where Patton's breakout created the situation Lüttich was designed to reverse; the two sites tell the same story from opposite sides.
- Falaise and Mont Ormel (60–80 km northeast): The eventual destruction of the forces that attacked at Mortain; completing the operational arc.

## Museums

- Mortain Memorial Museum (local): A small municipal museum in Mortain covering the Operation Lüttich fighting and the siege of Hill 317. Check local opening hours.

## Visitor notes

Mortain is a quiet hilltop town with compelling terrain. Hill 317 can be reached by a short drive and walk northeast of the town center; the wooded ridge is clearly identifiable as the ground the Lost Battalion held. Allow 2 hours. Combine with Avranches (Patton memorial) for a morning-afternoon pair that covers the breakout and the counterattack on the same day — the distance is 25 km.
