## Why this place matters

Cap de la Hague is where the Cotentin Peninsula tapers to its most exposed point, pushing out into the English Channel between the Gulf of Saint-Malo to the south and the Alderney Race (Raz Blanchard) to the north. The landscape here belongs entirely to the sea and the wind: low granite headlands, heathland covered in gorse and heather, tiny harbours sheltered behind breakwaters, and the channel between Alderney and the Hague — one of the fastest tidal flows in the world, running at up to 12 knots during spring tides.

The Nez de Jobourg is the most dramatic viewpoint on the Cotentin coast: a broad headland of dark schist and granite dropping 130 metres to the sea, with views extending to the Channel Islands on clear days. The coast road west of Cherbourg follows a succession of sea cliffs and tiny coves that see almost no tourism compared to the D-Day beaches 60 kilometres to the south.

## Natural features

- **Raz Blanchard (Alderney Race)**: The tidal channel between Cap de la Hague and Alderney runs at up to 12 knots at peak spring tides — among the strongest tidal flows in the world. Visible from the Nez de Jobourg as a line of churning white water even on calm days.
- **Nez de Jobourg cliffs**: 130 metres of near-vertical schist and granite, with nesting seabirds and dramatic sea stacks; the headland is accessible by a short walk from the car park.
- **Heathland**: The interior of the cape is covered in coastal heath with gorse, heather, and sheltered orchid-rich grassland — unusual in Normandy, which is dominated by bocage and pasture.
- **Goury lighthouse**: The red-and-white tower at the tip of the cape, accessible at low tide via a causeway, sits over the race; the lifeboat station here operates in some of the most demanding waters in Europe.

## Wildlife and best seasons

- **Seabirds**: Guillemots, razorbills, kittiwakes, and fulmars nest on the Jobourg cliffs; best observed in spring (April–June).
- **Cetaceans**: Bottlenose dolphins follow the tidal races through the channel; harbour porpoises are seen year-round from the headlands.
- **Heathland flowers**: The coastal heath is at its most colourful in late July and August (heather) and March–April (gorse).
- **Best weather**: Late spring and early autumn give the clearest visibility for Channel Island views and calmer seas; summer is reliable but can be misty.

## What to see nearby

- Nez de Jobourg viewpoint (central point on the cape): The essential stop; the 130-metre drop to the sea and the view over the race make this the finest coastal viewpoint on the Cotentin.
- Goury (at the cape tip): The tiny harbour with the tidal lighthouse; the contrast between the calm interior and the churning race visible beyond the breakwater is striking.
- Port Racine (1 km north of Goury): Claimed to be the smallest port in France — a single quay in a sheltered crevice, atmospheric at low tide.
- Cherbourg (30 km east): The port city whose WW2 capture required control of this entire peninsula; the Cité de la Mer maritime museum is there.
- Dunes de Biville (25 km south): The coastal dune reserve on the western Cotentin coast, a natural complement to the Hague's cliff landscape.

## Visitor notes

Cap de la Hague is best explored by car with short walks at each stop: Nez de Jobourg (30 minutes), Goury (15 minutes), Port Racine (15 minutes). A half-day circuit from Cherbourg west along the D901 coast road, ending at Goury, covers the full cape. The road through the interior heath (D401) is narrower and less visited than the coast road. Bring wind protection year-round — the Hague is fully exposed to Channel weather even on days that feel calm inland.
