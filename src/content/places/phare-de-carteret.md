## Why this place matters

Cap de Carteret and its lighthouse occupy the western edge of the Cotentin peninsula, overlooking the bay between Normandy and the Channel Islands. During the German occupation the cape's elevation made it valuable for coastal surveillance and as a reference point for Atlantic Wall defenses covering the western approaches to the peninsula.

In June 1944, as US VII Corps drove north after the Utah Beach breakout, the western Cotentin coast was progressively cut off. The fall of this stretch removed the last German observation and communication links on the peninsula's Atlantic side, helping seal the isolation of Cherbourg.

For WW2-focused visitors, the cape illustrates how the Cotentin campaign was as much about coastal control as inland fighting — and why the Germans placed defensive emphasis on the peninsula's perimeter as well as its port.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1940–1944: German forces integrated Cap de Carteret into Atlantic Wall coastal surveillance and light defense positions covering the western Cotentin and Channel Island approaches.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-14: Following Utah Beach, US VII Corps (First Army) expanded the Cotentin lodgment northward and westward against German Seventh Army resistance.
- 1944-06-15 to 1944-06-18: US divisions drove west across the peninsula toward Barneville-sur-Mer, severing the Cotentin and cutting off all German forces north of that line including those on the western coast.
- 1944-06-19 to 1944-06-27: With the peninsula sealed, US VII Corps concentrated on reducing the Cherbourg fortress; western coastal positions including the Carteret area passed under Allied control with little organized resistance.

## Important people

- Lt. Gen. J. Lawton Collins: Commanded US VII Corps; directed the westward cut across Cotentin that isolated the western coast.
- Maj. Gen. Robert C. Macon: Led US 83rd Infantry Division operations on the western Cotentin flank during the drive toward the sea.
- Gen. Karl-Wilhelm von Schlieben: German commander of the Cherbourg garrison; the isolation of positions like Cap de Carteret was part of the encirclement that made his position untenable.

## What to see nearby

- Cap de Carteret viewpoint: Wide views across the bay toward Jersey and the Channel Islands, illustrating the strategic observation value of the cape.
- Barneville-Carteret harbor area: The small port town provides context for the coastal geography the US advance sought to control.
- Cherbourg and Fort du Roule: The culminating objective of the Cotentin campaign, approximately 40 km north.
- Utah Beach Landing Museum: Gives operational background to how the beachhead fed the drive that reached this coast.
- Azeville and Crisbecq batteries: Atlantic Wall gun positions to the east that complement a western Cotentin coastal itinerary.

## Museums

- Liberation Museum at Fort du Roule (Cherbourg): The best regional museum for the June 1944 Cotentin campaign.
- Utah Beach Landing Museum: Strong operational context for the VII Corps advance.
- Airborne Museum (Sainte-Mere-Eglise): Explains the airborne operations that enabled the wider US maneuver across the peninsula.

## Visitor notes

Allow 1–2 hours for Cap de Carteret; the lighthouse and cape walk offer excellent views that help visualize the strategic geography. Pair with a Cherbourg visit or a western Cotentin loop via Barneville and the Atlantic Wall sites further north. The cape is accessible year-round but can be exposed and windy — check conditions before planning an extended outdoor visit.
