## Why this place matters

The Parc Naturel Régional des Marais du Cotentin et du Bessin was established in 1991 to protect a distinctive landscape that occupies the low-lying valleys of the Douve, Taute, and Vire rivers between the western beaches and the bocage interior. These are the same marshes — deliberately flooded by the Germans in 1944 by closing sluice gates at the coast — that turned the airborne landings into a nightmare of scattered sticks and men wading through waist-deep water in the dark.

Today the park covers nearly 150,000 hectares of wet grassland, reed beds, hedgerow bocage, and open water. It is one of the most important bird habitats in France, supporting up to 200,000 wading birds and wildfowl during autumn migration, and sustaining breeding populations of rare species including black-tailed godwit, corncrake, and whiskered tern. The landscape that caused such military catastrophe in 1944 is now protected specifically for the ecological value of its flooding.

## Natural features

- **Flooded meadows (prairies humides)**: The valley floors flood naturally from autumn to spring as the rivers spill their banks; the resulting shallow water attracts huge flocks of ducks, swans, and waders.
- **Bocage**: The hedgerow-divided fields that characterise the upland areas of the park are a landscape unique to Normandy and Brittany; the tall earth banks topped with oak and elm create a tunnel effect on minor roads and make navigation difficult — precisely the property that frustrated Allied armoured advances in 1944.
- **Reed beds**: Extensive beds of Phragmites australis along the river margins provide nesting habitat for marsh harrier, bearded tit, and reed warbler.
- **Maraîchins cattle**: A local breed of cattle adapted to the wet meadows, visible grazing in the flooded fields in winter.

## Wildlife and best seasons

- **Autumn migration (August–November)**: Peak season for wading birds; tens of thousands of golden plover, lapwing, dunlin, and snipe concentrate on the wet meadows; best viewed from hides near Carentan (Réserve Naturelle de Beauguillot) and along the D-Day causeway roads.
- **Spring (March–May)**: Breeding season for the park's rare waders; corncrake calling in wet grassland; marsh harrier displaying over reed beds.
- **Winter**: White-fronted geese and Bewick's swans from Russia use the flooded fields; barn owls hunting meadow margins at dusk.
- **Summer**: Quieter for birds but the bocage wildflowers are at their best; dragonflies abundant along the ditches.

## What to see nearby

- Réserve Naturelle de Beauguillot (near Saint-Germain-de-Varreville, 5 km from Utah Beach): One of the best bird-watching sites in Normandy; hides overlooking flooded fields that were part of the D-Day inundation zone; accessible on foot from the car park.
- Carentan (10 km south): The town the 101st Airborne captured to close the gap between Utah and Omaha — the marshy approach causeways are still clearly identifiable from the D514.
- La Fière Bridge (15 km northwest): The 82nd Airborne's Merderet River causeway; the flooded terrain that made La Fière so important is partially visible from the road.
- Parc Naturel visitor centre (Saint-Côme-du-Mont): Information on walking trails, canoe hire, and guided wildlife excursions into the marsh.

## Visitor notes

The park is best visited by car with short walks or stops at viewpoints and hides. The D-Day causeway roads (D913 from Carentan toward Utah, and D974) run directly through the inundation zone and give good overview of the flat marsh terrain. The Beauguillot reserve car park is signposted from Saint-Germain-de-Varreville. Canoe hire from Saint-Côme-du-Mont allows exploration of the marsh by water — the best way to understand the terrain the paratroopers waded through. Allow a half-day combining Beauguillot and the Carentan causeways with Utah Beach nearby.
