## Why this place matters

Arromanches-les-Bains sits at the center of Gold Beach and was selected as the site of Mulberry Harbor B ("Port Winston") because its bay offered natural protection from Channel weather. The artificial harbor was among the most ambitious engineering projects of the war: 146 reinforced concrete caissons (Phoenix units) up to 60 meters long and 18 meters high were sunk to form a breakwater, supplemented by floating steel pierheads (Lobnitz pontoons) and flexible roadways (Whale pontoons) that rose and fell with the tide.

The harbor became operational in mid-June 1944, just weeks after the landings, and by the time it was wound down it had landed over three million tons of supplies, 77,000 vehicles, and 500,000 troops. When a violent Channel storm on 19–22 June 1944 destroyed the American Mulberry A at Omaha Beach, the Arromanches harbor survived — partly because the Allies reinforced it with components from the wrecked American harbor — and it continued to operate until November 1944 by which time port facilities at Cherbourg, Antwerp, and Le Havre had come online.

Several of the Phoenix caissons are still clearly visible from the beach and the clifftop viewpoint above the town, making Arromanches one of the most visually tangible D-Day sites in Normandy.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06: Gold Beach assault secures Arromanches; the village is taken by the 1st Hampshire Regiment (231st Brigade, 50th Division) on D-Day afternoon.
- 1944-06-07 to 1944-06-18: Engineering units and Royal Navy personnel begin sinking the Phoenix caissons in the bay; Lobnitz pierheads and Whale floating roadways are assembled.
- 1944-06-19 to 1944-06-22: A severe Channel storm — the worst in 40 years for June — destroys Mulberry A at Omaha Beach entirely; Mulberry B at Arromanches sustains damage but survives, partly because its bay offers better shelter.
- 1944-06-27 onwards: Mulberry B reaches full operational capacity; peak throughput reaches approximately 7,000 tons per day.
- 1944-06 to 1944-11: Over the harbor's operational life, roughly 3 million tons of materiel, 77,000 vehicles, and 500,000 soldiers pass through Arromanches.

## Important people

- Rear Admiral William Tennant: Led the planning and assembly of the Mulberry harbors; oversaw the towing of Phoenix units across the Channel.
- Major General Sir Harold Wernher: One of the key figures in the engineering development of the Mulberry concept from its earliest COSSAC planning stages.
- Prime Minister Winston Churchill: Strong proponent of the artificial harbors from the earliest planning; the name "Port Winston" was adopted posthumously in his honor.
- Brigadier Bruce White: Royal Engineers officer centrally involved in the development of the Whale floating-roadway system.

## What to see nearby

- Mulberry Harbor B remains (visible from beach): At low tide the concrete caissons stand clearly above the waterline; the scale is easier to appreciate from the clifftop above the town than from sea level.
- Musée du Débarquement (in Arromanches): The original Mulberry Harbor museum; scale models, original film, and recovered harbor components explain the engineering.
- Arromanches 360 Circular Cinema (on the cliff above town): 20-minute film combining archive and contemporary footage in a 360-degree screen.
- Gold Beach sectors (east along coast): The assault beaches of the 50th Division are within 3 km.
- Longues-sur-Mer battery (5 km west): Well-preserved German coastal artillery position; guns still in casemates.

## Museums

- Musée du Débarquement Arromanches (in Arromanches): Opened 1954; original harbor models, recovered Phoenix components, a diorama of the harbor at full operation, and an excellent short film. Allow 45 to 60 minutes.
- Arromanches 360 Circular Cinema (clifftop): Runs every 30 minutes; worth 20 minutes before or after the main museum for the aerial perspective on the harbor remains.

## Visitor notes

Arromanches is a natural stop on any British beaches itinerary and very compact: museum, cinema, beach, and clifftop viewpoint can all be done in a half-day. Visit at low tide for the clearest view of the caissons. The clifftop viewpoint above the 360 cinema gives the best sense of how the harbor sat in the bay. Combine with Bayeux (10 km south) and Gold Beach sectors for a full British sector day.
