## Why this place matters

The British Normandy Memorial is the newest major commemorative site in Normandy, opened on 6 June 2021 — 77 years after D-Day — by Prince Charles and President Macron. It was built to address a long-standing gap: while the United States has the Normandy American Cemetery and the Commonwealth has the Bayeux War Cemetery, Britain lacked a dedicated national memorial at the beaches themselves that named all its dead from the campaign.

The memorial was designed by architect Liam O'Connor on a commanding hilltop at Ver-sur-Mer, directly overlooking Gold Beach — the British sector landing zone. Its colonnades bear the names of 22,442 British service personnel killed between D-Day on 6 June 1944 and the end of the Battle of Normandy on 31 August 1944: soldiers, sailors, and airmen from the Army, Royal Navy, and Royal Air Force. The site also includes a sculpture by David Williams-Ellis and a garden designed to be maintained year-round.

The memorial's elevated position on the Ver-sur-Mer ridge gives it panoramic views over Gold Beach and the Channel, making the geography of the landing immediately apparent.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06: British Second Army (including XXX Corps at Gold Beach and I Corps at Sword Beach) and supporting Royal Navy and RAF formations conduct the British landing operations that the memorial commemorates.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-08-31: The full scope of British casualties in Normandy — 22,442 killed — encompasses the D-Day landings, the Caen campaign, the bocage fighting, and the breakout through to the end of the Normandy operational period.
- 2021-06-06: Memorial inaugurated; attended by survivors, veterans' families, and the heads of state of France and the United Kingdom.

## Important people

- King George VI: Symbolic head of the British armed forces throughout the Normandy campaign; a dedication to his memory forms part of the memorial's inscription.
- General Bernard Montgomery: Commander British 21st Army Group; responsible for all Allied ground forces in Normandy; his strategy shaped the campaign that the memorial's casualties reflect.
- Liam O'Connor: Architect of the memorial; his design uses colonnades and a central statue of soldiers to frame the view over Gold Beach.

## What to see nearby

- Gold Beach (directly below, 2 km north): The British landing beach the memorial overlooks; the coastal road runs through the assault sectors.
- Arromanches (3 km west): Mulberry Harbor B site with museum and the 360-degree cinema.
- Bayeux War Cemetery (12 km southwest): The largest Commonwealth cemetery in Normandy; complements the memorial as the primary burial site.
- America Gold Beach Museum (Ver-sur-Mer, adjacent): Small museum covering the Gold Beach assault in the same village.
- Longues-sur-Mer battery (6 km west): German coastal artillery position with guns still in casemates.

## Museums

- British Normandy Memorial (Ver-sur-Mer): The site itself is the primary feature; interpretation panels and a visitor information point are on site. The surrounding grounds are free to visit. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour to walk the colonnades, read the inscriptions, and take in the view over Gold Beach.
- America Gold Beach Museum (Ver-sur-Mer, adjacent): Covers the Gold Beach assault; small ticket required.

## Visitor notes

The British Normandy Memorial is free to visit and is best combined with a Gold Beach and Arromanches day. The hilltop location is exposed to Channel wind — dress accordingly in cooler months. The view over Gold Beach from the colonnade terrace is the most powerful element: standing above the beach where the 50th Division landed while reading the names of the dead alongside gives the site its full weight. Allow 45 minutes to 1 hour.
