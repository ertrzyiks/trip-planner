## Why this place matters

Étretat sits on the Alabaster Coast (Côte d'Albâtre), the stretch of the Norman coast east of Le Havre where the chalk plateau of the Pays de Caux meets the English Channel in a series of vertical white cliffs up to 80 metres high. The town itself is a small resort, but it is framed by two headlands — the Falaise d'Amont to the northeast and the Falaise d'Aval to the southwest — each sculpted by wave action into arches, needles, and caves that make the site unlike anything else on the French coast.

The cliffs entered the public imagination through the Impressionists: Monet painted the Falaise d'Aval more than 40 times, and the site attracted Courbet, Delacroix, Maupassant, and later Pablo Picasso. The combination of white chalk, dark grey pebble beach, and the pierced headlands at either end creates an unusually dramatic natural composition that holds up well in person after the familiarity of reproductions.

Étretat lies about 80 kilometres east of the D-Day beaches — a significant detour — but is included here as the most exceptional coastal landscape within reasonable range of a Normandy itinerary.

## Natural features

- **Falaise d'Aval**: The western headland, with the Porte d'Aval arch (the largest of the three Étretat arches), the standing needle rock (L'Aiguille), and a second arch (Manneporte) visible from the cliff path; the chalk here shows clear geological layering of the Cretaceous period.
- **Falaise d'Amont**: The eastern headland with a smaller arch and the chapel of Notre-Dame de la Garde; quieter than d'Aval and with a better overview of the whole bay.
- **Pebble beach**: Étretat has no sand — the beach is entirely large flint pebbles (galets), the same flint that is abundant in the surrounding chalk plateau; the pebble shelf drops steeply to the water, making swimming rougher than it appears.
- **Chalk geology**: The white chalk visible in the cliffs is Upper Cretaceous (approximately 70–90 million years old); the flint nodules embedded in it are formed from silicified marine sponges; the same chalk formation continues under the English Channel to the Dover cliffs.

## Wildlife and best seasons

- **Seabirds**: Northern gannets can be seen offshore from the clifftops, particularly in autumn during their southward migration; fulmars nest in the cliff faces.
- **Spring**: The clifftop grassland above both headlands blooms with chalk-loving wildflowers (cowslip, orchids) in April–May; the cliffs are least crowded.
- **Summer**: Very popular July–August; the beach and cliff paths are busy but the late evening light on the chalk is worth staying for.
- **Autumn and winter**: Reduced crowds; dramatic light when the chalk glows orange in low sun; occasional rough seas that show the erosive force that created the arches.

## What to see nearby

- Cliff path circuit (Étretat): The path over the Falaise d'Aval and back along the shore road to d'Amont is a 3–4 km walk with exceptional views; a longer circuit continues west to the third arch, Manneporte, and the Antifer lighthouse.
- Les Jardins d'Étretat (above the town): Modern garden with coastal views; sculptural installation in a converted park.
- Fécamp (20 km northeast): Another Alabaster Coast town with a notable Benedictine abbey and a maritime museum.
- Le Havre (30 km south): Major port city rebuilt by Auguste Perret after WW2 destruction; listed as a UNESCO World Heritage site for its post-war architecture.
- Honfleur (50 km southwest): Historic harbour town at the mouth of the Seine, associated with the Impressionist painters who visited Étretat.

## Visitor notes

Allow 2 to 3 hours to walk both headlands. Start on the Falaise d'Aval side (the famous arch), climb the cliff path to the top, walk along the clifftop to the Manneporte if time allows, then return and cross to Falaise d'Amont for the overview. The town is small and compact; restaurant options are concentrated on the main street. Parking fills by mid-morning in summer — arrive before 09:30 or after 17:00. The pebble beach is uncomfortable for sitting but fine for walking to the base of d'Aval at low tide.
