## Why this place matters

The Normandy American Cemetery is situated on a 70-hectare plateau at Colleville-sur-Mer, on land donated in perpetuity by France and administered as consecrated American soil by the American Battle Monuments Commission (ABMC). Established on 8 June 1944 as the first American cemetery on European soil in this war, its current permanent form was completed between 1948 and 1956 after families chose whether to repatriate remains or consent to permanent burial in France.

The 9,387 grave markers — white Carrara marble Latin crosses and Stars of David — face west toward the United States. Among those buried here are 33 pairs of brothers interred side by side, and 307 unknowns marked "Here rests in honored glory a comrade in arms known but to God." The cemetery holds soldiers from across the Normandy campaign and beyond, not only those who fell on Omaha Beach below.

The visitors' center, opened in 2007, is one of the most thorough D-Day interpretive facilities in Normandy. Standing at the bluff's edge and looking down onto Omaha Beach while reading the scale of the losses above is one of the most affecting experiences the Normandy battlefield offers.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-08: First American cemetery on European soil in World War II established at this location by US Army Graves Registration personnel.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-08-25: Casualties from across US First Army operations — Omaha and Utah sectors, the Cotentin drive, the Caen flank, and the breakout — contribute to the burial population.
- Post-war: Reorganization and construction of the permanent cemetery completed by 1956; American sculptor Donald De Lue's bronze sculpture *The Spirit of American Youth Rising from the Waves* placed as the central memorial.

## Important people

- General Theodore Roosevelt Jr. (buried here): Son of President Theodore Roosevelt; awarded the Medal of Honor for his leadership at Utah Beach on 6 June 1944; died of a heart attack on 12 July 1944, age 56; the only Medal of Honor recipient buried in the cemetery.
- Brigadier General Stanhope Mason (buried here): Chief of Staff, 1st Infantry Division; died July 1944.
- The Niland brothers: Three of four brothers were reported killed in action in the Normandy period; the surviving brother Fritz Niland was sent home under the Sole Survivor policy — the story that inspired the fictional Ryan family in *Saving Private Ryan*.
- General George C. Marshall: US Army Chief of Staff; worked with ABMC after the war to establish the permanent cemeteries and secure their long-term maintenance.

## What to see nearby

- Omaha Beach (directly below, 3-minute walk from the overlook): Walk the beach access path for an immediate understanding of the ground the soldiers crossed.
- WN62 strongpoint (eastern Omaha, within walking distance): One of the best-preserved German bunker complexes on the beach.
- Pointe du Hoc (14 km west): Ranger assault site preserving original craters and casemates.
- Overlord Museum (adjacent to cemetery grounds): Large armored vehicle and equipment collection.
- Colleville-sur-Mer church: Contains a window honoring the American dead.

## Museums

- ABMC Visitors Center (on site): Free admission; permanent exhibition covers D-Day planning, the assault, personal stories of the buried, and the post-war cemetery construction. The orientation film is essential viewing before walking the grounds. Allow at least 1.5 hours for the center alone.
- Overlord Museum (adjacent, separate ticket): Focuses on vehicles and equipment recovered from across Normandy; largest collection of armored vehicles in France.

## Visitor notes

The cemetery is open year-round and free of charge. It receives over one million visitors per year; arriving before 09:00 is significantly quieter and more reflective. The walk from the visitors' center through the memorial garden to the grave rows, then to the overlook above Omaha Beach, takes at least 90 minutes at a respectful pace. Graves can be looked up in advance on the ABMC website; staff can provide sand and paper for grave rubbings on request. Dress conservatively — it is consecrated ground.
