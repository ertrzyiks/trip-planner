## Why this place matters

The Juno Beach Centre is Canada's national D-Day memorial and museum, opened in 2003 at Courseulles-sur-Mer on the western sector of the Canadian landing beach. It is the only Canadian presence on the five D-Day beaches and was built to ensure that the Canadian contribution to the Normandy campaign — often overshadowed by the American and British narratives — is properly interpreted and remembered.

The centre sits directly on Juno Beach, with access to the beach through the gardens. It covers not only the D-Day assault by the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division but the full scope of Canadian war participation: the home front, the Royal Canadian Navy and Royal Canadian Air Force contributions, the Italian campaign, and the Dieppe Raid of 1942 that shaped Canadian tactical thinking by D-Day.

The garden of provinces and territories, surrounding the building, includes stones and plants from each of Canada's provinces and territories — a deliberate connection between the national landscape and the soldiers buried in France.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06: 3rd Canadian Infantry Division (I Corps, British Second Army) lands on Juno Beach; the Queen's Own Rifles, Royal Winnipeg Rifles, Regina Rifles, North Shore Regiment, and other units suffer approximately 1,200 casualties but advance further inland than any Allied formation on D-Day.
- 1944-06 to 1944-08: Canadian forces under First Canadian Army fight through the Caen sector, conduct Operations Totalize and Tractable toward Falaise, and close the Falaise Pocket with the Polish 1st Armoured Division.
- 2003: Juno Beach Centre opens; built and funded by Canadian veterans, families, and the Canadian government as a permanent national memorial at the site of the landing.

## Important people

- Garth Webb: Canadian veteran and the driving force behind the Juno Beach Centre's creation; began fundraising in the 1990s and saw the centre open in 2003.
- Major General Rod Keller: Commander 3rd Canadian Infantry Division on D-Day; led the Juno landing.
- General H.D.G. Crerar: Commander First Canadian Army; commanded all Canadian forces in northwest Europe from July 1944 through the end of the war.

## What to see nearby

- Juno Beach itself (directly accessible from the centre): The beach access path leads through the dunes to the actual landing beach; German bunkers are still visible at the waterline.
- Bernières-sur-Mer (3 km east): Best-preserved Juno Beach village; "Canada House" building visible in iconic D-Day photographs still stands on the seafront.
- Bény-sur-Mer Canadian War Cemetery (3 km south on D35): 2,048 Canadian graves; the most important Canadian cemetery in Normandy.
- Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer (5 km east): Preserved WN27 German casemate on the beach at the eastern end of Juno.
- Courseulles-sur-Mer harbor: Where a Sherman tank recovered from the sea floor stands as a memorial on the quayside.

## Museums

- Juno Beach Centre (Courseulles-sur-Mer): Permanent exhibitions on the D-Day assault, Canadian military contributions across the war, and individual stories of the 45,000 Canadians who served in the Normandy campaign. Guided beach walks are available in season. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours indoors; add 30 to 45 minutes for a beach walk and the surrounding German fortifications.

## Visitor notes

The Juno Beach Centre is the natural starting point for any visit to Juno Beach; the building and gardens orient visitors before they walk onto the beach. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours indoors, then walk east along the seafront toward Bernières and Saint-Aubin for the best-preserved beach landscape and German fortifications. The Bény-sur-Mer Canadian War Cemetery is 3 km south on the D35 and adds 30 to 45 minutes. The full Juno circuit makes a rewarding half-day.
