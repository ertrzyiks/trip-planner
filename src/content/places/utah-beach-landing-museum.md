## Why this place matters

The Utah Beach Landing Museum sits directly on the beach at Sainte-Marie-du-Mont, at the southern end of the D-Day landing zone — close to the spot where Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr. made his famous decision to advance from the wrong sector. It is the essential museum for understanding the Utah Beach landing and the 101st and 82nd Airborne operations that preceded and supported it.

The museum was significantly expanded and reopened in 2011 around its centerpiece: a complete B-26 Marauder medium bomber recovered from the English Channel, where it had lain since being shot down during the D-Day bombardment. The aircraft — named "Thunderbird" — is one of only a handful of intact B-26s surviving in the world. Alongside it, an LCVP (Higgins boat) landing craft, a Sherman tank, and period vehicles provide the ground-level view of the landing.

Unlike museums focused on narrative panels alone, Utah Beach Landing Museum uses its large collection of recovered and preserved equipment to give visitors a visceral sense of the scale and complexity of the amphibious operation.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~06:30: 4th Infantry Division (VII Corps, US First Army) lands at Utah; the museum occupies the ground of the actual landing sector.
- 1944-06-06: 101st and 82nd Airborne Divisions drop inland during the night; paratroopers secure the causeways behind the beach that allow 4th Division to move inland.
- 1944-06-06: Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr. makes the famous "we'll start the war from right here" decision near the museum's current location.
- Post-war: Artifacts recovered from the seabed and surrounding area are progressively acquired; the B-26 Marauder "Thunderbird" is raised from the Channel and becomes the museum's centrepiece after the 2011 expansion.

## Important people

- Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr.: His decision to advance from the wrong sector is directly tied to the museum's location; buried at the Normandy American Cemetery; the museum's surrounding terrain marks the ground he walked on D-Day morning.
- Major General J. Lawton Collins: Commander VII Corps; his planning and execution of the Utah landing and the subsequent Cotentin drive is a central museum theme.
- General Maxwell Taylor: Commander 101st Airborne Division; the airborne–seaborne coordination is comprehensively covered in the museum's exhibits.

## What to see nearby

- Utah Beach itself (directly outside): The monument marking the landing and the 4th Division memorial are on the beach access road immediately adjacent.
- Sainte-Mère-Église (12 km north): The 82nd Airborne's principal objective; the Airborne Museum there is the strong companion to Utah Beach Landing Museum for the seaborne and airborne narrative.
- Pointe du Hoc (18 km southeast): The Ranger cliff assault that threatened both invasion beaches; connects the two US landing beaches.
- Azeville and Crisbecq batteries (7–12 km north): Well-preserved Atlantic Wall artillery positions that threatened the Utah sector before being overrun.
- La Madeleine liberation stele: The stone marker at the beach identifies the approximate landing area.

## Museums

- Utah Beach Landing Museum (Sainte-Marie-du-Mont, on the beach): The B-26 Marauder "Thunderbird," LCVP Higgins boat, Sherman M4A1 tank, and M7 Priest self-propelled gun are the major hardware exhibits; interpretive galleries cover the planning of the Utah landing, the airborne operations, and the Cotentin drive through Cherbourg. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

## Visitor notes

The Utah Beach Landing Museum is consistently rated among the best D-Day museums in Normandy; it earns a full 1.5 to 2 hours. The B-26 Marauder alone is worth the visit for aviation history enthusiasts. Combine with Sainte-Mère-Église (12 km north) for the airborne side of the same operation — the two together make an excellent full US airborne-seaborne day. The beach outside the museum is publicly accessible and the monument area is compact enough to walk in 20 minutes.
