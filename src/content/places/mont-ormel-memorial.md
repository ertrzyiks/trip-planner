## Why this place matters

Mont Ormel — Hill 262, called "La Maczuga" (the Mace) by the Polish soldiers who held it — is a prominent wooded ridge northeast of Chambois overlooking the Dives valley. In mid-August 1944, as the Falaise Pocket contracted and tens of thousands of German troops tried to escape east through the narrowing corridor, the Polish 1st Armoured Division seized the hill and held it for three days against furious German counterattacks.

The position gave the Poles — and the Allied artillery they directed — observation over the main escape route through the valley below. Surrounded and running short of food, water, and ammunition, they refused to be dislodged. German soldiers who had fought across the Soviet Union, North Africa, and all of France were throwing themselves against the Polish positions in desperation. Polish gunners and tank crews destroyed hundreds of vehicles on the roads visible from the summit. When US and Canadian forces finally broke through to relieve the Poles on 21 August, the valley below was filled with destroyed German equipment and tens of thousands of dead soldiers and horses.

The memorial and museum on the summit are among the most moving lesser-known sites in Normandy, and the view from the hill makes the tactical situation immediately comprehensible.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-08-18 to 1944-08-19: 1st Polish Armoured Division (II Canadian Corps, First Canadian Army), commanded by General Stanisław Maczek, drives south and seizes Hill 262 (Mont Ormel) and Hill 262 South (Coudehard) overlooking the Chambois escape corridor.
- 1944-08-19: Polish forces on the hill are surrounded as German forces trying to break out of the pocket attack from east and west simultaneously; the Poles direct artillery fire onto escape routes visible in the valley below, destroying hundreds of vehicles and killing thousands.
- 1944-08-19 to 1944-08-21: Three days of siege; the Polish garrison — roughly 2,000 men — repels repeated attacks by elements of 2nd SS Panzer Division, Leibstandarte, and other German formations; Polish casualties are severe but the hill is held.
- 1944-08-21: US 90th Infantry Division and Canadian forces push through from the south and west to relieve Mont Ormel; the gap is effectively closed; an estimated 50,000 Germans are captured in the pocket, 10,000 killed.
- 1944-08-21: Polish and US forces link up at Chambois in the valley below Hill 262, formally closing the Falaise Pocket.

## Important people

- General Stanisław Maczek: Commander 1st Polish Armoured Division; the only senior Polish commander of his generation never captured or killed during WW2; died in Edinburgh in 1994 aged 102; buried among his soldiers at the Polish war cemetery at Breda, Netherlands.
- Lieutenant Colonel Aleksander Stefanowicz: Commander of the Polish battlegroup on Hill 262 during the three-day siege.
- General Guy Simonds: Commander II Canadian Corps (First Canadian Army); directed the overall operations that placed the Poles on Mont Ormel.

## What to see nearby

- Chambois village (5 km southwest): Where Polish and US forces linked up on 21 August; a memorial marks the junction.
- Falaise Pocket Museum (Chambois): Focused on the gap's closure from ground level.
- Falaise (25 km northwest): The town that gave the pocket its name; the Musée Août 1944 provides the big-picture context.
- Argentan (20 km west): The southern jaw of the pocket and site of the controversial Bradley halt order.

## Museums

- Mémorial de Montormel (on summit): Museum opened in 1994 dedicated to the Polish division's stand and the closing of the Falaise Gap; exhibits cover the siege, personal accounts from Polish veterans, and the strategic context of the battle. The terrace overlooks the valley where the German columns were destroyed. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours.

## Visitor notes

Mont Ormel Memorial is one of the most rewarding and undervisited WW2 sites in Normandy. The museum is small but excellent, and the terrace view over the valley where the German army was destroyed is genuinely affecting. Allow 1.5 hours. Combine with Chambois village (5 km downhill) and Falaise (25 km northwest) for a full Falaise Pocket day. The site is most powerful in the morning when the valley is clear of haze.
