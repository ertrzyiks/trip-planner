## Why this place matters

Bayeux War Cemetery holds 4,144 Commonwealth war dead and is the largest Second World War Commonwealth cemetery in France. It sits on the western edge of Bayeux, a city that holds the particular distinction of being the first significant Norman city liberated after D-Day — taken by the 50th (Northumbrian) Infantry Division on 7 June 1944 without a major fight, and largely undamaged as a result.

Opposite the cemetery stands the Bayeux Memorial, listing the names of 1,808 Commonwealth soldiers who have no known grave from the Normandy and northwest Europe campaigns — soldiers lost in the Channel, in the bocage, or in circumstances that left no recoverable remains. The cemetery and memorial together form one of the most important Commonwealth commemorative sites in Normandy, complementing the more visited Normandy American Cemetery by representing the British, Canadian, Australian, and other Allied dead who do not have a beach-side counterpart of equivalent scale.

The cemetery is administered by the Commonwealth War Graves Commission (CWGC) and is free to visit year-round.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-07: Bayeux is liberated by the 50th (Northumbrian) Infantry Division (XXX Corps, British Second Army) without major urban fighting — preserving the city's medieval center intact; it becomes the first base for British Second Army headquarters in France.
- 1944-06-07 onwards: Allied field hospitals are established in and around Bayeux; the city serves as a main British logistics and medical hub for the Normandy campaign throughout the summer of 1944.
- 1944 (campaign period): Casualties from across the British and Commonwealth sectors are concentrated here; the cemetery reflects the full sweep of the campaign from D-Day through the liberation of Normandy.
- Post-war: Permanent cemetery constructed and consecrated by the Commonwealth War Graves Commission; burials include men from the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, Poland, and other Allied nations.

## Important people

- General Bernard Montgomery: Commander British 21st Army Group; established his tactical headquarters in the Bayeux area in the first weeks after D-Day; briefly addressed liberated French citizens from Bayeux in June 1944.
- General Miles Dempsey: Commander British Second Army; used Bayeux as his rear headquarters area during the Caen campaign.
- Charles de Gaulle: Visited Bayeux on 14 June 1944 — his first return to French soil since 1940 — addressing a crowd in front of the prefecture; the reception confirmed that the Free French narrative of liberation had public legitimacy in France.

## What to see nearby

- Bayeux city center and the Tapestry (15 minutes walk): The Bayeux Tapestry Museum holds the 11th-century embroidery depicting the Norman Conquest; the medieval city center survived the campaign intact.
- British Normandy Memorial (12 km northeast, Ver-sur-Mer): The modern British national memorial opened in 2021 naming 22,442 British servicemen killed during the Normandy campaign.
- Arromanches (10 km north): Mulberry Harbor B site; natural companion for a full British sector day.
- Gold Beach sectors (12 km north): The landing beaches of the 50th Division that liberated Bayeux are a short drive north.
- Tilly-sur-Seulles War Cemetery and battlefield (10 km southeast): The bocage fighting that raged south of Bayeux for weeks after D-Day.

## Museums

- Musée d'Art et d'Histoire Baron Gérard (Bayeux): Art and history museum in the former bishop's palace; limited WW2 content but gives context for the city's history.
- Musée Mémorial de la Bataille de Normandie (Bayeux, Boulevard Fabian Ware): The main Bayeux WW2 museum; covers British forces in Normandy from D-Day through the liberation; good companion to the cemetery. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours.

## Visitor notes

Bayeux is one of the most rewarding single days in Normandy because it combines WW2 history with a largely intact medieval city. Start at the Tapestry Museum (book ahead in peak season), walk through the cathedral and city center, then visit the Battle of Normandy Museum and the war cemetery and memorial on the western edge of town. Allow a full day. Gold Beach and Arromanches are 12–15 km north for an afternoon extension.
