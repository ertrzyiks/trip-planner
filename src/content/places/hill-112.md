## Why this place matters

Hill 112 is an unremarkable-looking plateau southwest of Caen that rises only 112 meters above sea level — but from its summit in 1944, the Caen plain spread out in every direction and whoever held it could observe and direct fire across a vast area. Both sides understood what it meant, and the hill became one of the most intensively fought-over pieces of ground in the entire Normandy campaign.

British forces first seized the hill on 28 June 1944 during Operation Epsom, the attempt by VIII Corps to outflank Caen by crossing the Odon and Orne rivers. The 11th Armoured Division pushed tanks to the summit — briefly giving British commanders a view over the Orne toward Caen — before being forced back by a German counterattack. The hill then changed hands repeatedly through Operations Jupiter (10 July), Greenline, and subsequent British offensive actions, with the German II SS Panzer Corps — including the Leibstandarte, Das Reich, Hohenstaufen, and Frundsberg SS Panzer Divisions — defending it against the British 43rd (Wessex) Infantry Division and supporting armour.

The fighting around Hill 112 cost the combatants tens of thousands of casualties over six weeks. Soldiers called it "the hill of a thousand dead." A Churchill tank still stands on the summit as a memorial.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-26 to 1944-07-01: Operation Epsom — British VIII Corps (15th Scottish Division, 11th Armoured Division) crosses the Odon and seizes Hill 112 briefly; II SS Panzer Corps counterattacks force a withdrawal to the northern slopes; British hold a bridgehead but cannot exploit it.
- 1944-07-10: Operation Jupiter — British 43rd (Wessex) Infantry Division attacks Hill 112; 5th Duke of Cornwall's Light Infantry and 4th Somerset Light Infantry reach the summit repeatedly but are driven off by German armor and artillery.
- 1944-07-10 to 1944-08-04: Continuous British pressure and German counterattacks; the hill changes hands several times at a cost of thousands of casualties on both sides; German 9th SS Panzer Division (Hohenstaufen) and 10th SS Panzer Division (Frundsberg) defend throughout July.
- 1944-08-04: Hill 112 is finally and permanently secured by British and Canadian forces as the German position in the Caen sector collapses ahead of Operation Totalize.

## Important people

- Lieutenant General Richard O'Connor: Commander British VIII Corps; conducted Operation Epsom and the initial push for Hill 112; one of the most experienced British commanders, having been captured in North Africa and escaped.
- General Paul Hausser: Commander II SS Panzer Corps; organized the SS Panzer counterattacks that repeatedly threw British forces off the hill; historians disagree on the precise coordination of unit attacks.
- Major General Ivor Thomas: Commander 43rd (Wessex) Infantry Division; directed the brutal July fighting for the hill including Operation Jupiter.

## What to see nearby

- Hill 112 memorial and Churchill tank (on site): A preserved Churchill tank marks the summit; the 43rd Wessex Division memorial stands nearby; a stone Celtic cross commemorates the Cornish soldiers who fought here.
- Caen Memorial Museum (12 km northeast): The Mémorial provides campaign-wide context that situates the Hill 112 fighting in Montgomery's broader strategy.
- Tilly-sur-Seulles (15 km west): Similarly brutal bocage fighting from an earlier phase of the Caen battle; connects the June–July sequence.
- Odon valley (below the hill): The river crossing that VIII Corps forced during Operation Epsom; visible from the hill.

## Museums

- Hill 112 Memorial Site (on the summit plateau): Not a museum building but an outdoor memorial site with interpretation panels, the Churchill tank, and the 43rd Division and other regimental memorials. Free to visit; no facilities. Allow 30 to 45 minutes on site.
- Caen Memorial Museum (12 km northeast): The best contextual resource for understanding the British strategy that made Hill 112 so important; see the Caen section.

## Visitor notes

Hill 112 is a quiet, often-overlooked site that rewards historians willing to leave the beach circuit. The summit plateau gives an immediate understanding of why the hill was so important: on a clear day Caen and the Orne valley are visible to the northeast, and the entire operational landscape opens up. Allow 45 minutes on the summit; combine with Tilly-sur-Seulles and the Caen Memorial Museum for a full British campaign day away from the beaches.
