## Why this place matters

Utah Beach occupies the southeastern tip of the Cotentin Peninsula, where the US VII Corps landed as part of Operation Neptune on 6 June 1944. The beach was the westernmost of the five D-Day landing zones and the only one within range of the 101st and 82nd Airborne Divisions dropped inland the night before to secure the exits.

Strong currents pushed the first wave roughly two kilometers south of the intended landing sector — an error that inadvertently placed the assault troops in a sector with fewer German defenses and lighter obstacles. Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr., the only general officer to land with the first wave on D-Day, assessed the situation and gave the order to attack from the new position: "We'll start the war from right here." The decision avoided a bloody fight to reorient the assault and allowed the beach to be secured with fewer than 200 killed — the lightest losses of any D-Day beach.

Utah's success was critical to the overall operation because it opened the avenue for VII Corps to drive north up the Cotentin Peninsula and capture the port of Cherbourg, which the Allied logistical plan depended on.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~06:30: Lead elements of the US 4th Infantry Division (8th Infantry Regiment, VII Corps, US First Army) land approximately 2 km south of the planned sector due to smoke, wind, and current; the accidentally chosen stretch is far less heavily defended.
- 1944-06-06 ~06:30–09:00: Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr. leads reconnaissance of the new landing area and orders the entire assault to proceed from the shifted position; follow-on units redirected accordingly.
- 1944-06-06 morning: 4th Infantry Division links up with airborne units from the 101st Airborne Division at the causeways leading inland, securing the beach exits against German 709th Infantry Division defenders.
- 1944-06-06: Approximately 23,000 men and 1,700 vehicles land at Utah by end of D-Day; US casualties estimated at 197 killed — the lowest of any beach.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-12: VII Corps expands the Utah beachhead northward and westward, linking up with the broader Cotentin drive toward Cherbourg.

## Important people

- Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr.: Assistant commander 4th Infantry Division; the only general officer to go ashore in the first wave on D-Day; awarded the Medal of Honor for his leadership at Utah; died of a heart attack on 12 July 1944; buried at the Normandy American Cemetery.
- Major General J. Lawton Collins: Commander US VII Corps; coordinated the Utah landing with the airborne operations that secured the causeways.
- General Maxwell Taylor: Commander 101st Airborne Division; his paratroopers seized the inland causeways essential to the Utah Beach breakout.
- General Wilhelm Falley: Commander German 91st Infantry Division; killed in his car on 6 June 1944 while driving toward the sound of the airborne drop — one of the first German general officers killed in the Normandy campaign.

## What to see nearby

- Utah Beach Landing Museum (on the beach): The primary interpretive site for the Utah sector; includes a B-26 Marauder and landing craft.
- Sainte-Mère-Église (12 km north): First town liberated in France; covers the 82nd Airborne drop that preceded the landing.
- Pointe du Hoc (18 km southeast): Ranger cliff assault complementing the Utah narrative.
- Azeville and Crisbecq batteries: Major Atlantic Wall artillery positions that threatened the Utah sector before being silenced by airborne and infantry action.
- La Madeleine and the liberation memorial stele: The spot on the beach where Roosevelt made his famous decision to advance from the wrong sector.

## Museums

- Utah Beach Landing Museum (Utah Beach, Sainte-Marie-du-Mont): The best museum for the Utah sector; exhibits a B-26 Marauder bomber, LCVP landing craft, Sherman tank, and detailed coverage of the airborne-seaborne link-up. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.
- Airborne Museum (Sainte-Mère-Église): Covers 82nd Airborne operations that directly supported the Utah landing, including a C-47 and Waco glider under the same roof.

## Visitor notes

Utah Beach is less crowded than Omaha and rewards a slower visit. The Utah Beach Landing Museum is among the best-curated D-Day museums in Normandy and should not be skipped in favor of just walking the beach. Allow half a day here, then combine with Sainte-Mère-Église (30 minutes north) for a full US airborne-seaborne day. The beaches are publicly accessible and the dunes behind the landing zones preserve the Atlantic Wall geography well.
