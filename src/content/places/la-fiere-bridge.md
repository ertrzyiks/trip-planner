## Why this place matters

La Fière is a tiny hamlet 3 km west of Sainte-Mère-Église where a narrow stone bridge and causeway cross the flooded Merderet River. In June 1944 the causeway was one of only a handful of crossings that could allow forces to move between the eastern and western Cotentin — making it an objective of immediate importance to both sides.

The 82nd Airborne Division's scattered drop left small groups of paratroopers fighting to take and hold the bridge from the early hours of 6 June. The position was seized, lost, and retaken across D-Day before a coherent defense was established. Then, for three days, the Americans held it against German counterattacks across an open causeway with the flooded Merderet marshes on either side — an assault corridor where there was nowhere to hide and no way to outflank. The Germans threw infantry and armor at the position repeatedly. The paratroopers held with machine guns, antitank weapons, and artillery called in at dangerously close range.

The bridge was finally crossed in strength on 9 June, opening the western Cotentin to the 82nd Airborne and VII Corps. A bronze statue — "Iron Mike," depicting a charging paratrooper — stands at the bridge today as one of the most powerful ground-level memorials in Normandy.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~04:00: Scattered elements of the 505th and 507th Parachute Infantry Regiments (82nd Airborne Division) fight their way to the La Fière bridge; the bridge changes hands multiple times through the morning.
- 1944-06-06 midday: A mixed 82nd Airborne force under Lieutenant John "Red Dog" Dolan secures the eastern end of the bridge and establishes a defensive perimeter.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-08: German forces — primarily 1057th and 1058th Grenadier Regiments (91st Infantry Division) with armor support — mount repeated counterattacks across the causeway; the paratroopers repel each attack at close range; accurate artillery support from the USS Quincy and shore-based guns is critical.
- 1944-06-09 morning: The 325th Glider Infantry Regiment reinforces the position and crosses the causeway in a set-piece assault under fire; the western Merderet bank is secured; the La Fière causeway is held at a cost of approximately 650 US casualties over three days.

## Important people

- Lieutenant John "Red Dog" Dolan: Commander A Company, 505th PIR; organized the initial defense of the bridge on D-Day and held command of the position through the first day's fighting.
- General James M. Gavin: Assistant commander 82nd Airborne Division (later commander); present at La Fière on D-Day, personally directing the fight for the bridge; his account in *On to Berlin* is the primary senior officer's narrative of the action.
- Lieutenant Colonel Charles Timmes: Commander 2nd Battalion, 507th PIR; his battalion was cut off west of the Merderet but held a perimeter near Amfreville that drew German forces away from the bridge.
- General Matthew Ridgway: Commander 82nd Airborne Division; ordered the decisive crossing on 9 June after three days of stalemate.

## What to see nearby

- Iron Mike statue (at the bridge): The bronze paratrooper memorial erected after the war; the most photographed WW2 ground-level monument in the Cotentin; the bridge and causeway itself is visible from the statue.
- Sainte-Mère-Église (3 km east): The 82nd Airborne's primary town objective and the Airborne Museum; natural first stop before La Fière.
- Chef-du-Pont (1 km south): The secondary Merderet crossing where the 507th PIR fought simultaneously; a smaller memorial marks the site.
- Carentan (25 km south): The 101st Airborne's parallel causeway battle; together the two sites show how the entire Cotentin airborne plan pivoted on narrow water crossings.
- Utah Beach (15 km east): The seaborne force whose link-up with the airborne justified holding La Fière at such cost.

## Museums

- Airborne Museum (Sainte-Mère-Église, 3 km east): The primary museum for 82nd and 101st Airborne operations; the C-47 and Waco glider exhibits provide direct context for the scattered drop that defined the La Fière fight. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.

## Visitor notes

La Fière takes 30 to 45 minutes: walk from the Iron Mike statue to the bridge itself and look back at the causeway the Germans attacked across. The flooded marsh is now drained but the low flat ground on both sides of the narrow road makes the defensive problem immediately clear. Combine with Sainte-Mère-Église (3 km east, 30 minutes) and Chef-du-Pont (1 km south, 15 minutes) for a complete Merderet crossing circuit before or after Utah Beach.
