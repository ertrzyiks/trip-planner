## Why this place matters

Avranches sits at the southwestern tip of the Normandy bocage where the land opens toward Brittany and the Loire valley. In the last days of July 1944, after weeks of attritional fighting in the hedgerows, this city became the gateway for the most dramatic operational transformation of the western campaign.

Operation Cobra — the US breakout launched on 25 July — punched through the German line west of Saint-Lô, and by 30–31 July General J. Lawton Collins's VII Corps had reached Avranches. The town sat at the top of a narrow coastal corridor with the sea on one side and, briefly, a collapsing German line on the other. General George Patton, whose US Third Army had just been activated on 1 August, sent his armored divisions through Avranches at night in a single 24-hour period — sometimes across a single bridge controlled by a single US officer who waved 15,000 vehicles through in darkness.

The Avranches "gap" was the hinge on which the entire Allied strategy rotated: US forces could now swing east toward Paris or west into Brittany, and the German high command's attempt to close the gap at Mortain only accelerated the destruction of its forces in the Falaise Pocket.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-07-25 to 1944-07-30: Operation Cobra — US First Army (VII Corps, VIII Corps) ruptures the German line west of Saint-Lô; US armored divisions exploit southward faster than German reserves can respond.
- 1944-07-30 to 1944-07-31: US 4th Armored Division (VIII Corps) reaches Avranches and seizes the town; the vital bridge at Pontaubault, 8 km south, is captured intact — the only crossing of the See River in range.
- 1944-08-01: US Third Army (General Patton) is officially activated; VIII Corps transfers to Third Army control; seven divisions pour through the Avranches–Pontaubault corridor in the next 48 hours, turning west into Brittany and east toward the Seine.
- 1944-08-07 to 1944-08-13: German Operation Lüttich — Hitler orders a counterattack west toward Avranches to cut off the American forces already east and south; the attack at Mortain stalls under Allied air power and US ground resistance; the counterattacking forces are then trapped in the Falaise Pocket.

## Important people

- General George S. Patton: Commander US Third Army; his activation on 1 August and the subsequent exploitation through Avranches was the operational breakthrough that turned the Normandy campaign into pursuit; a monument to Patton stands on the hill above Avranches.
- General J. Lawton Collins: Commander VII Corps (US First Army); directed the Cobra breakout that opened the Avranches corridor.
- Brigadier General John S. Wood: Commander US 4th Armored Division; drove the exploitation that reached Avranches and seized the Pontaubault bridge.
- General Omar Bradley: Commander US 12th Army Group (from 1 August); the Avranches corridor gave him the operational opportunity to shift Patton east and encircle the German forces rather than drive west into Brittany.

## What to see nearby

- Patton Monument (Avranches city center, on the Jardin des Plantes viewpoint): Memorial to Patton with a panoramic view over the bay toward Mont Saint-Michel.
- Pontaubault bridge (8 km south): The bridge Patton's forces crossed; the current structure replaced the wartime span but the location is identifiable.
- Mont Saint-Michel (22 km west): The iconic tidal island visible from Avranches; was briefly in the combat zone in August 1944 but survived the war intact.
- Mortain (25 km east): Site of the German counterattack (Operation Lüttich) that Patton's breakthrough provoked; connect the two sites to understand the German strategic failure.
- Saint-Lô (35 km north): The "capital of ruins" whose capture made Cobra possible; Avranches is the logical endpoint of the Saint-Lô to Avranches campaign arc.

## Museums

- US Patton Memorial (on the hill viewpoint, Avranches): Small open-air memorial rather than a museum; the view over the bay and bocage is the main draw.
- Scriptorial d'Avranches (Avranches): Manuscript museum with no WW2 focus, but the city's hilltop center shows the geography Patton's forces exploited.

## Visitor notes

Avranches is worth a 2-hour stop built around the Patton monument and the hilltop viewpoint — the view over the Baie du Mont Saint-Michel is exceptional and gives a strong sense of the strategic geography Patton exploited. Combine with Mont Saint-Michel (a major site in its own right regardless of WW2 interest) and Mortain for a full southwestern Normandy day that covers both the breakout and the German counterattack.
