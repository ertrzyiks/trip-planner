## Why this place matters

Omaha Beach is the defining image of D-Day. On the morning of 6 June 1944, two US infantry divisions and attached Ranger units crossed five kilometers of open sand under murderous fire from bluffs held by a veteran German division. Preliminary bombing missed its targets entirely, most amphibious DD tanks sank in the rough sea before reaching shore, and the assault companies — landing in the wrong sectors after strong currents pushed the flotilla east — faced uncut wire, intact bunkers, and enfilading machine-gun fire with almost no armored support.

The result was close to catastrophe on the western sectors. A Company of the 116th Infantry Regiment was effectively destroyed within minutes of landing at Dog Green. Yet by late afternoon, small groups of soldiers led by individual officers and NCOs had crawled off the beach, climbed the bluffs through gaps and draws, and begun to encircle the strongpoints from behind. The German defensive line, though never completely overrun from the front, began to collapse from flanking pressure and naval gunfire that came dangerously close to US troops.

By nightfall, US forces held a shallow, fragile beachhead. It had cost more American lives in a single day than any other D-Day sector, but the beach was held and the lodgment survived.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~06:30: First waves of the US 1st Infantry Division (right sector, Easy Red to Fox Green) and 29th Infantry Division (left sector, Dog Green to Easy Green) land at Omaha; most DD Sherman tanks of the 741st Tank Battalion sink in heavy seas.
- 1944-06-06 ~06:30–08:00: A Company, 116th Infantry Regiment (29th ID) suffers near-total casualties at Dog Green; assault boats are destroyed or pinned and the attack stalls across multiple sectors.
- 1944-06-06 ~08:00: Destroyers of the US Navy come within a few hundred meters of shore to provide direct-fire support, risking grounding to suppress bunkers.
- 1944-06-06 ~08:30: Brigadier General Norman "Dutch" Cota (29th ID) and Colonel George Taylor (1st ID) personally lead men off the beach in separate sectors; Taylor gives the famous order: "Two kinds of people are staying on this beach, the dead and those who are going to die — now let's get the hell out of here."
- 1944-06-06 midday–evening: US infantry infiltrate the bluffs through draws and gullies, attacking strongpoints from the flanks and rear; organized German resistance on the high ground begins to collapse sector by sector.
- 1944-06-06: German 352nd Infantry Division and attached Ost-battalion units defend all six sectors (WN60–WN73); US casualties estimated at 1,200–2,000 killed, wounded, or missing.

## Important people

- Brigadier General Norman "Dutch" Cota: Assistant commander 29th Infantry Division; rallied pinned troops at the Dog sector and was instrumental in getting men off the beach.
- Colonel George A. Taylor: Commander 16th Infantry Regiment (1st ID); gave the famous order that helped break the paralysis on Easy Red.
- Colonel Charles Canham: Commander 116th Infantry Regiment (29th ID); wounded early but continued to lead, helping drive inland movement.
- General Omar Bradley: Commander US First Army; monitored the disaster from USS Augusta and briefly considered abandoning Omaha — the beach's survival owed largely to the actions of soldiers on the ground.
- General Dietrich Kraiss: Commander German 352nd Infantry Division; his division's presence at Omaha was not detected by Allied intelligence until D-Day itself.

## What to see nearby

- Normandy American Cemetery and Memorial (3 km east, Colleville-sur-Mer): 9,387 graves overlooking the beach; the single most visited American memorial in France.
- Pointe du Hoc (14 km west): Ranger cliff assault site, preserved with original craters and casemates.
- WN62 bunker complex (on the beach at the eastern end): One of the best-preserved German strongpoints directly on Omaha; the Ruquet Valley approach is visible.
- Overlord Museum (Colleville-sur-Mer): Large collection of armored vehicles and equipment adjacent to the American Cemetery.
- Les Moulins draw and Vierville draw: The key exits from the beach where US forces broke through; both are accessible by road and show the terrain challenge clearly.

## Museums

- Normandy American Cemetery Visitor Center (Colleville-sur-Mer): Free admission; extensive film, maps, and personal accounts covering the Omaha assault and the wider V Corps battle. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours.
- Overlord Museum (Colleville-sur-Mer): Armor-focused; unique collection of US and German vehicles. Ticket required.
- D-Day Omaha Museum (Vierville-sur-Mer): Smaller private collection near Dog Green sector; covers Ranger and 29th Division equipment.
- Memorial Museum of Omaha Beach (Saint-Laurent-sur-Mer): Located close to the beach access ramp; focuses on US equipment and the assault narrative.

## Visitor notes

Omaha is best visited starting from the American Cemetery and working westward along the beach road toward Vierville. Allow a full day: the cemetery, the beach itself, and at least one draw climb to understand why men died in such numbers below those bluffs. The beach is most evocative at low tide, when the full width of the killing ground is exposed. Combine with Pointe du Hoc as a second day or a long single-day itinerary with an early start.
