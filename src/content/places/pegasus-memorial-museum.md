## Why this place matters

The Mémorial Pegasus opened in 2000, 200 meters east of the current Pegasus Bridge, to house the original 1934 Bénouville swing bridge that was replaced in 1994. The original span — the one D Company, 2nd Ox and Bucks Light Infantry, stormed at 00:16 on 6 June 1944 — was preserved because it was the first Allied objective captured on D-Day and because veterans of the assault campaigned for its retention. The museum was built around the bridge to give it proper context.

The collection focuses tightly on Operation Tonga — the 6th Airborne Division's operations on the eastern flank — and specifically on the D Company assault that secured both the Caen Canal bridge and the Orne River bridge in the first minutes of D-Day. Major John Howard's personal jeep, weapons used in the assault, maps, letters, and the accounts of the men who landed in three Horsa gliders in the dark give the museum an intimacy that larger facilities rarely achieve.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 00:16: D Company, 2nd Bn Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire Light Infantry (6th Airborne Division), lands by glider and seizes the Caen Canal bridge intact.
- Post-1944 (wartime): The bridge is renamed "Pegasus Bridge" by the Airborne Forces after the divisional insignia of the 6th Airborne.
- 1994: A modern bridge replaces the original 1934 swing span; the original structure is preserved pending construction of a permanent museum.
- 2000: Mémorial Pegasus opens at Ranville; the original bridge is reassembled inside the museum building as the central exhibit.

## Important people

- Major John Howard: Commander D Company; central figure in the assault; his personal accounts form much of the museum's interpretive backbone; died in 1999, shortly before the museum opened.
- Sergeant Jim Wallwork: Pilot of the lead glider; his navigational precision in complete darkness made the assault possible; awarded the Distinguished Flying Medal.
- Warlene Gondrée: Daughter of Georges and Thérèse Gondrée, owners of the Café Gondrée beside the bridge — the first house liberated in France; the café is still operated by the family and is 200 meters from the museum.

## What to see nearby

- Pegasus Bridge itself (200 m west): Walk to the current bridge; the original approach ramp and the Café Gondrée are immediately visible.
- Ranville War Cemetery (400 m southeast): First Allied war cemetery established in France; contains the grave of Lieutenant Den Brotheridge, the first Allied officer killed in ground combat on D-Day.
- Horsa Bridge (500 m east): The companion bridge over the Orne River, also seized in the same operation; less visited but accessible on foot.
- Sword Beach / Ouistreham (8 km north): The beach Lord Lovat's Commandos marched from to relieve the bridges.
- Merville Gun Battery (12 km northeast): The other major 6th Airborne coup-de-main objective on the same night.

## Museums

- Mémorial Pegasus (Ranville): Displays the original bridge span, a reproduction Horsa glider nose section, Major Howard's jeep, weapons, maps, personal letters, and a film covering the operation. Closed in January; check hours for peak season. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours.

## Visitor notes

Visit the museum before walking to the bridge itself — the context makes the few hundred meters of ground between the museum and the Café Gondrée far more legible. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours in the museum, then 30 minutes at the bridge and café. Ranville War Cemetery is a short walk and contains Den Brotheridge's grave in plot B, row B. The full site — museum, bridge, cemetery — takes about 2.5 hours.
