## Why this place matters

Argentan sits at the southern edge of the Falaise basin and in August 1944 it became the southern limit of the greatest Allied encirclement attempt of the western campaign. After Patton's Third Army broke out of Avranches and swung east, General Haislip's XV Corps drove north through Le Mans and reached the outskirts of Argentan by 13 August 1944. With Canadian forces pressing south toward Falaise from the north, the two Allied forces were barely 25 kilometers apart — and an enormous German force was trapped between them.

General Omar Bradley then halted Patton's advance at the inter-army boundary line that ran through Argentan, refusing to allow XV Corps to push north and close the gap. Bradley's reasons — concern about a collision with Canadian forces, the risk of encircling more Germans than his forces could handle, and respect for established boundaries — have been debated ever since. The Germans used the open gap to extract a significant portion of their forces before it was finally closed at Chambois on 21 August. Estimates suggest 20,000 to 40,000 Germans escaped the pocket, though 50,000 were captured and 10,000 killed inside.

Argentan itself was heavily damaged in the fighting and bombing, and was finally taken on 20 August 1944 primarily by the French 2nd Armored Division.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-08-10 to 1944-08-13: US XV Corps (General Haislip, US Third Army) drives north through Le Mans and approaches Argentan; French 2nd Armored Division and US 5th Armored Division reach the outskirts; Argentan is within reach.
- 1944-08-13: General Bradley orders Haislip to halt at the Argentan–Sées line; Patton protests but is ordered to stop short of closing the gap; the decision allows German forces to continue withdrawing through the Chambois corridor.
- 1944-08-14 to 1944-08-19: Fighting around Argentan continues as German forces attempt to hold open the southern escape route; US forces hold the line while Canadians press from the north.
- 1944-08-20: Argentan is taken by the French 2nd Armored Division; the town is heavily damaged.
- 1944-08-21: The Falaise Pocket is formally closed at Chambois, 20 km east of Argentan, when Polish and US forces link up.

## Important people

- General Wade Haislip: Commander XV Corps (US Third Army); drove the exploitation that reached Argentan; urged Bradley to allow him to continue north but was halted.
- General George Patton: Commander US Third Army; famously frustrated by the halt order at Argentan; his forces could have closed the pocket from the south but were restrained.
- General Omar Bradley: Commander US 12th Army Group; ordered the halt; his reasoning — including fear of friendly-fire incidents with Canadian forces — remains contested by historians.
- General Philippe Leclerc: Commander French 2nd Armored Division; his division took Argentan on 20 August and later led the liberation of Paris.

## What to see nearby

- Falaise (20 km north): The northern jaw of the pocket; the Musée Août 1944 covers the full encirclement story.
- Mont Ormel Memorial (20 km northeast): Hill 262, where the Polish 1st Armoured Division physically closed the gap; the memorial overlooks the escape corridor.
- Chambois (20 km east): The village where the gap was finally sealed on 21 August; a small memorial marks the Polish–American link-up.
- American Military Cemetery at Saint-James (90 km west): A less-visited American cemetery with 4,410 graves from the Cobra and Third Army campaigns.

## Museums

- Musée Août 1944 (Falaise, 20 km north): The principal museum for the Falaise Pocket battle; covers both the northern and southern pincers including the controversy around Argentan.
- Falaise Pocket Museum (Chambois, 20 km east): Focused on the gap's closure; located at the actual site of the 21 August link-up.

## Visitor notes

Argentan is a working Norman town with limited WW2 interpretation on its own; the main reason to visit is the drive connecting it to Falaise (north) and Chambois / Mont Ormel (east) for the full pocket battlefield. If time is short, prioritize Mont Ormel Memorial over Argentan itself — the hilltop gives the best overall view of the escape corridor and the terrain the Germans fought through in August 1944.
