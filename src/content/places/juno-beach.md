## Why this place matters

Juno Beach was assigned to the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division under I Corps (British Second Army) — the only predominantly Canadian formation to land on D-Day. The beach runs between Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer in the east and Courseulles-sur-Mer in the west, covering a frontage of about eight kilometers. Canadian forces had been preparing for an invasion landing since the disastrous Dieppe Raid of August 1942, and Juno was their opportunity to prove the lessons learned from that catastrophe.

The landing was costly. Offshore reefs forced the assault to be delayed 10 minutes, allowing the tide to rise and cover the beach obstacles — many landing craft were holed or sunk on mines and stakes before reaching shore. The villages of Bernières-sur-Mer and Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer were defended by well-prepared strongpoints that caused heavy casualties in the initial waves. Yet within hours, the Canadians had broken through and were pushing inland faster than any other Allied formation. By evening the 3rd Canadian Division had advanced between eight and eleven kilometers inland, reaching the outskirts of Carpiquet airfield — the deepest penetration of any D-Day force.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~07:35–08:05: 3rd Canadian Infantry Division (I Corps, British Second Army) lands at Juno; Royal Winnipeg Rifles and Regina Rifles attack the western sectors (Mike sector, Courseulles); Queen's Own Rifles of Canada and North Shore (New Brunswick) Regiment attack the eastern sectors (Nan sector, Bernières, Saint-Aubin); DD tanks of the 1st Hussars and Fort Garry Horse provide some armored support.
- 1944-06-06 ~07:45: Queen's Own Rifles land at Bernières-sur-Mer and suffer devastating losses approaching the seawall under machine-gun fire — the single company taking the heaviest casualties of any unit at Juno.
- 1944-06-06 ~08:00–11:00: Strongpoints at Courseulles, Bernières, and Saint-Aubin are taken by combined infantry and tank action; German 716th Infantry Division defending Juno suffers serious losses.
- 1944-06-06 afternoon: 9th Canadian Infantry Brigade pushes inland; lead elements of the North Nova Scotia Highlanders reach the outskirts of Carpiquet airfield by evening — 11 km from the coast.
- 1944-06-06: Canadian casualties estimated at approximately 1,200 killed, wounded, and missing — the second highest percentage behind Omaha.

## Important people

- Major General Rod Keller: Commander 3rd Canadian Infantry Division; led the Juno assault; controversially wounded by friendly fire in August 1944 and replaced.
- Lieutenant Colonel John Matheson: Led the Regina Rifles at Courseulles; one of the key regimental commanders at Juno's western sector.
- Major Elliott Dalton: Led A Company, Queen's Own Rifles, at Bernières; suffered catastrophic losses in the first minutes but pressed the attack onto the seawall.
- Lieutenant General John Crocker: Commander I Corps (British Second Army); responsible for coordinating the Juno and Sword landings.

## What to see nearby

- Juno Beach Centre (Courseulles-sur-Mer): The Canadian national memorial and museum at the beach; the essential stop for understanding the Canadian perspective.
- Bernières-sur-Mer: The best-preserved Juno Beach village; the seawall and beach approach still show the geography of the assault; the "Canada House" building — visible in iconic D-Day photos — still stands.
- Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer: Small resort town at the eastern end of Juno; a 50mm gun casemate (WN27) survives at beach level as a preserved strongpoint.
- Bény-sur-Mer Canadian War Cemetery: The main Canadian cemetery for Juno Beach casualties, 3 km inland; 2,048 graves.
- Carpiquet airfield (10 km south): The objective the Canadians nearly reached on D-Day but that cost weeks of fighting to finally secure in July 1944.

## Museums

- Juno Beach Centre (Courseulles-sur-Mer): The primary Canadian memorial and museum at Juno; covers the Battle of Normandy, Canadian war effort, and personal stories of the 45,000 Canadians who served in the campaign. Allow 1.5 to 2 hours. Guided beach walks available in season.

## Visitor notes

Allow at least half a day for Juno Beach. Start at the Juno Beach Centre in Courseulles, then drive east along the coast road to Bernières-sur-Mer (walk the beach access and look at "Canada House"), then to Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer to see the preserved WN27 casemate. The Bény-sur-Mer Canadian War Cemetery is 3 km inland on the D35 and takes 30 to 45 minutes. The whole circuit is manageable in an efficient day combined with Gold or Sword Beach.
