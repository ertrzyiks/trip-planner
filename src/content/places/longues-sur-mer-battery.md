## Why this place matters

Longues-sur-Mer sits on the cliffs between Arromanches and Port-en-Bessin, exactly at the junction of the British and American D-Day sectors. Its four 152mm Czech-manufactured naval guns — mounted in massive reinforced concrete casemates — had a range of over 20 kilometers, theoretically capable of reaching both Gold Beach to the east and Omaha Beach to the west simultaneously. Allied planners rated it one of the most dangerous batteries on the entire Norman coast.

On D-Day morning, Longues-sur-Mer opened fire on the Allied fleet at around 05:37 — among the earliest German coastal batteries to engage. HMS Ajax, the French cruiser Georges Leygues, and USS Arkansas directed counter-battery fire at the position through the morning. By afternoon the battery had been partially neutralized, with several casemates damaged and fire sporadic. The German garrison of 184 men surrendered on 7 June to men of the 231st Brigade (50th Division) advancing from Gold Beach.

What makes Longues-sur-Mer unique among Normandy's many coastal fortifications is completeness: all four gun casemates survive with their weapons still in place, the fire-control bunker on the cliff edge is intact, and the whole site is maintained as an open-air monument. No other battery in Normandy retains this combination of original guns and panoramic coastal position.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 ~05:37: Longues-sur-Mer battery opens fire on the Allied invasion fleet; German Marine-Artillerie-Abteilung 1260 operates the four guns.
- 1944-06-06 morning: HMS Ajax (Royal Navy), Georges Leygues (French Navy), and USS Arkansas engage in counter-battery fire; several shells penetrate the casemates and damage gun mechanisms; battery fire becomes intermittent.
- 1944-06-06 evening: Battery falls silent; German garrison intact but guns partially disabled.
- 1944-06-07: Men of the 231st Infantry Brigade (50th Division, British Second Army), advancing inland from Gold Beach, accept the surrender of the 184-man garrison without significant resistance.

## Important people

- Admiral Bertram Ramsay: Allied Naval Commander-in-Chief (Operation Neptune); responsible for the naval fire plans that assigned counter-battery missions to ships opposite Longues-sur-Mer.
- Captain John Lee (HMS Ajax): Commanded HMS Ajax during the D-Day bombardment of Longues-sur-Mer; the cruiser's fire silenced the battery during the critical morning hours.
- General Erwin Rommel: As Army Group B commander, inspected the Longues-sur-Mer position in the months before D-Day and pushed for its strengthening; the heavy casemates reflected his influence on Atlantic Wall construction.

## What to see nearby

- Fire-control bunker (cliff edge, on site): The original observation and fire-direction post perched on the cliff above the sea, with the invasion panorama still visible from its embrasures; provides an exact view of what the German gunners could see on D-Day morning.
- Arromanches (3 km east): Mulberry Harbor B site; the harbor remains are visible from the Longues cliff edge.
- Port-en-Bessin (5 km west): The 47 Royal Marine Commando objective taken on 7 June; the two sites together cover the British-American junction.
- Gold Beach sectors (4 km east along coast road): The landing beach the Longues battery threatened from the flank.
- Omaha Beach (14 km west): The American beach that Longues guns could theoretically range.

## Museums

- Longues-sur-Mer Battery (on site): Open-air monument maintained by the Calvados department; no admission charge; interpretation panels at each casemate explain the gun type, crew, and D-Day engagement. Allow 45 to 60 minutes to walk all four casemates and the cliff-edge fire-control post.

## Visitor notes

Longues-sur-Mer is one of the most rewarding 45-minute stops in Normandy — compact, free, and completely authentic. Walk the casemates in order from west to east, ending at the cliff-edge fire-control post for the view over the invasion coast. Visit in the morning when the light comes from the east and the sea is clear. Natural combination with Arromanches (3 km east) and Port-en-Bessin (5 km west) for a British-sector half-day, or as a midpoint stop between Omaha and Gold beaches.
