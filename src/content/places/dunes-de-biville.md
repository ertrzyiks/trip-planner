## Why this place matters

The dune system at Biville occupies a 4-kilometre stretch of the western Cotentin coast, sheltered from the prevailing Atlantic swell by the Cap de la Hague to the north and rising directly from a narrow beach to crests up to 80 metres above sea level — among the highest coastal dunes in France. The dunes are still mobile: the prevailing west wind moves them eastward year by year, slowly engulfing the grass and heath at their landward edge.

The site has been a protected nature reserve since 1975 and is managed jointly by the Conservatoire du Littoral and the regional authorities. Access is restricted to marked paths to protect the vegetation that stabilises the dune face: a complex community of marram grass, sand sedge, sea rocket, and further back into the dune system, rare orchids and specialised moss communities that need undisturbed sand to establish.

For visitors driving the western Cotentin coast between Carteret (the lighthouse) and Cap de la Hague, Biville is the most dramatic natural stopping point on the route — a landscape of pale sand and dark Atlantic horizon unlike anything in the bocage or the beach sectors.

## Natural features

- **Mobile dunes**: The active face of the dune moves eastward at approximately 1 metre per year; the advancing sand overtops and buries vegetation, leaving ghostly grey trunks of engulfed shrubs visible in the dune face.
- **Dune slack vegetation**: The hollow areas between dune ridges (slacks) hold moisture and support a different plant community than the exposed crests; rare orchids (including marsh helleborine) grow in the slacks.
- **Blow-outs**: Wind erosion creates bowl-shaped hollows (blow-outs) in the dune face where the sand is exposed and mobile; these are the most visually dramatic features on the crest.
- **Coastal views**: From the dune tops, the view west extends over the Gulf of Saint-Malo toward the Channel Islands; on clear days, Guernsey and Jersey are visible.

## Wildlife and best seasons

- **Plants**: Sand dune specialists including sea holly, Portland spurge, and sand cat's-tail; rare species include curved hard-grass and dune gentian. Best in late spring (May–June) before summer heat desiccates the vegetation.
- **Insects**: The south-facing dune faces warm rapidly and support thermophilic species including sand wasps, tiger beetles, and several rare spider species.
- **Birds**: Little tern nest on the beach south of the dunes (protected breeding area June–August); stonechat and linnet breed in the dune heath.
- **Best access season**: Spring and early summer for flora; autumn for fewer visitors and dramatic light; winter for the full experience of the exposed coast.

## What to see nearby

- Phare de Carteret (20 km south): The lighthouse on Cap de Carteret, already in the trip planner, with its dramatic headland walk; the coast road between Carteret and Biville follows the western Cotentin shore.
- Cap de la Hague (25 km north): The wild northwest tip of the peninsula; Biville sits at the midpoint between Carteret and the Hague, making a natural one-day coastal driving circuit.
- Flamanville (3 km south): Small coastal village; also the site of the controversial Flamanville nuclear power station visible from the dune crest to the south.
- Portbail (25 km south): Tidal estuary with the widest beach on the western Cotentin; good for walking at low tide.
- Cherbourg (35 km north): The port city and D-Day campaign objective; the natural endpoint of a western Cotentin drive that starts at Carteret, passes Biville, and ends at the Hague.

## Visitor notes

Park at the designated car park on the D117 and follow the marked path to the dune crest — the path climbs steeply but reaches the top in about 10 minutes. Stay on the marked paths; leaving them damages the crust that stabilises the dune face. From the crest, a longer circular route (about 3 km) runs north along the dune top before descending to the beach. Allow 1 to 1.5 hours. Combine with Phare de Carteret (20 km south) for a half-day western Cotentin coast loop.
