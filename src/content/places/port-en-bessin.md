## Why this place matters

Port-en-Bessin is a small fishing harbor sitting between two cliff headlands at the boundary between the British and American invasion sectors — Gold Beach lay 10 km to the east, Omaha Beach 12 km to the west. The port was operationally important for two reasons: it was the only sheltered natural harbor available in the initial lodgment area, and it marked the junction between the two Allied army groups, a boundary that needed to be physically closed to prevent German forces from exploiting the gap.

The assignment went to 47 Royal Marine Commando, which landed at Gold Beach on D-Day and immediately set off westward on foot. The column fought through several German positions on the march, losing men before even reaching its objective. On the night of 6–7 June, the Commandos assaulted the harbor from the clifftops above, fighting down into the town and through the German strongpoints that covered the harbor entrance. The port fell on 7 June after close-quarters fighting that cost the unit roughly a third of its strength.

Post-capture, Port-en-Bessin became the French terminus of PLUTO — Pipe Line Under The Ocean — which by mid-1944 was pumping fuel from England directly to the Normandy beachhead. The harbor the Commandos died for sustained Allied operations for months.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 afternoon: 47 Royal Marine Commando (1st Special Service Brigade) lands at Gold Beach (La Rivière sector) and begins its 10 km march westward toward Port-en-Bessin, engaging German positions en route and sustaining casualties.
- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-07: The Commandos consolidate at a forming-up position on the high ground east of Port-en-Bessin and prepare the assault plan.
- 1944-06-07 ~21:00: 47 Commando attacks the German strongpoints on both cliff headlands (WN56 and WN57) simultaneously; fighting continues through the night on the cliffs and in the town below.
- 1944-06-07 ~23:00: Port-en-Bessin is secured; approximately 300 German prisoners taken; 47 Commando has lost over 200 men killed and wounded from its original strength of around 420 during the march and assault.
- 1944-06-12 onwards: US–British junction formally established; Port-en-Bessin becomes the logistics link between the sectors.
- Mid-1944: PLUTO (Pipe Line Under The Ocean) fuel pipeline from England connects to Port-en-Bessin, delivering aviation fuel and motor fuel directly to the Allied armies.

## Important people

- Lieutenant Colonel Charles Frederick Phillips: Commanding officer 47 Royal Marine Commando; directed the march from Gold Beach and the cliff assault on Port-en-Bessin; wounded during the operation.
- Admiral Bertram Ramsay: Allied Naval Commander-in-Chief; closing the British-American boundary at Port-en-Bessin was a key element of his Neptune plan.

## What to see nearby

- Longues-sur-Mer Battery (5 km east): The Atlantic Wall coastal artillery position with original guns in casemates; the battery that was neutralized to protect the Gold Beach sector the Commandos marched from.
- Omaha Beach (12 km west): The American beach that Port-en-Bessin's capture finally linked to Gold Beach.
- WN56 and WN57 cliff positions (above the harbor): The German strongpoints 47 Commando assaulted; the cliff paths are walkable and show the ground the Commandos climbed.
- Arromanches (13 km east): Mulberry Harbor B; the logistics context that gives Port-en-Bessin's capture its full significance.
- Bayeux (10 km south): The first liberated city; natural endpoint for a British sector day that includes Port-en-Bessin and Longues.

## Museums

- OVERLORD Museum (Colleville-sur-Mer, 12 km west): The closest major museum to Port-en-Bessin; armored vehicles and equipment from across the Normandy campaign.
- Port-en-Bessin itself: No dedicated museum, but the town's geography — two cliff headlands framing a narrow harbor entrance — makes the tactical problem of the assault immediately legible from the quayside or the cliff paths.

## Visitor notes

Port-en-Bessin is a working fishing port and one of the least-visited significant D-Day sites in Normandy. Walk the cliff path on the east headland (above the former WN57 position) for the best view of the harbor entrance and the ground 47 Commando fought over. Allow 30 to 45 minutes. Combine naturally with Longues-sur-Mer Battery (5 km east) and Arromanches (13 km east) for a half-day British sector itinerary.
