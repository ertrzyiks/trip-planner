## Why this place matters

Cherbourg was one of the most important logistical objectives after the Normandy landings. Allied planners needed a major deep-water port to sustain the breakout from the beachheads, and Cherbourg was the closest large port in reach of US forces on the Cotentin Peninsula.

The battle for Cherbourg formed the climax of the US drive up Cotentin after D-Day. Even though German demolition teams heavily damaged the harbor before surrendering, capturing the city denied the Axis a strong northern anchor and gave the Allies a port that could later support the advance across France.

For WW2 visitors, Cherbourg links frontline combat, urban fighting, coastal fortifications, and the wider logistics story of Operation Overlord in one compact area.

## Key WW2 events and armies

- 1944-06-06 to 1944-06-18: US First Army (including US VII Corps) secured Utah Beach lodgment and pushed north through Cotentin against German Seventh Army formations.
- 1944-06-19 to 1944-06-21: US VII Corps isolated the peninsula by cutting across to the west coast near Barneville-Carteret, trapping Cherbourg's garrison.
- 1944-06-22 to 1944-06-26: US 4th, 9th, and 79th Infantry Divisions (VII Corps) attacked Cherbourg's outer defenses against German fortress and garrison troops under local naval-army command.
- 1944-06-26: Major organized resistance in Cherbourg city collapsed; US forces entered and secured central sectors.
- 1944-06-27: Lt. Gen. J. Lawton Collins formally accepted the surrender of Gen. Karl-Wilhelm von Schlieben and Adm. Walter Hennecke as fighting ended in remaining pockets.
- 1944-06-28 to 1944-07-01: Mopping-up operations continued at nearby fortified positions, while Allied engineers began restoring a heavily sabotaged harbor.

## Important people

- Lt. Gen. J. Lawton Collins: Commander of US VII Corps, directed the Cotentin campaign and capture of Cherbourg.
- Gen. Karl-Wilhelm von Schlieben: German army commander of the Cherbourg fortress area during the final defense.
- Adm. Walter Hennecke: German naval commander involved in port defense and demolition preparations.
- Gen. Omar Bradley: Commander of US First Army, responsible for the broader operational plan that prioritized seizure of Cherbourg.

## What to see nearby

- Fort du Roule: Hilltop fortress and commanding viewpoint over the port; useful for understanding German defensive geography.
- Batterie du Roule and surrounding bunkers: Remains of fortified positions tied to harbor defense.
- Utah Beach sector (day trip): Connects the landing phase to the later push toward Cherbourg.
- Sainte-Mere-Eglise: Key airborne objective that helped secure approaches into Cotentin.
- Azeville and Crisbecq batteries: Major Atlantic Wall artillery sites illustrating coastal defense doctrine.

## Museums

- Liberation Museum at Fort du Roule (Musee de la Liberation): Focuses on the battle for Cherbourg and local occupation-liberation history inside a former fortification.
- Cite de la Mer: Maritime museum in the former transatlantic terminal; broader naval focus with useful context on Cherbourg as a strategic port.
- Airborne Museum (Sainte-Mere-Eglise): Strong companion museum for understanding how airborne operations shaped the peninsula campaign.

## Visitor notes

Plan at least half a day in Cherbourg itself: Fort du Roule plus harbor-area interpretation gives the best military-geography overview. Add a full day if combining with Utah Beach and Sainte-Mere-Eglise to follow the campaign arc from airborne-landings to port seizure.
